* Abstract

Recent surveys document growing dissatisfaction with the accounting
publication process: researchers believe acceptance rates should nearly
double and that top journals increasingly favor narrow methodologies. The
conventional explanation is reviewer bias. But the statistical properties of
peer review---specifically, low inter-rater reliability combined with
multi-dimensional conjunctive evaluation---produce systematic dysfunction
that disadvantages quality research even without bias. Using a simulation
calibrated to empirical reliability data (ICC = 0.34), I show that at
realistic selectivity levels, approximately 45% of deserving papers are
rejected due to measurement error alone. This error structure creates
perverse incentives: researchers maximize expected publications by producing
multiple moderate-quality papers rather than single excellent papers. The
problem is not that reviewers are biased. The problem is that the system is
structurally noisy---and that distinction points toward very different
remedies.

* Introduction

Ask an accounting professor whether good papers get rejected from top
journals and the answer is immediate: of course they do. Everyone knows
this. The field wisdom is that a good paper has roughly a coin-flip chance
of acceptance---and the data bears this out.
cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016 found that accounting academics
perceived very low citation patterns relative to other disciplines and
believed the review process was deteriorating.
cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 updated this survey and found
perceptions have only worsened: the rising generation of scholars holds even
more negative views than their predecessors, respondents believe acceptance
rates should nearly double, and there is widespread concern that top
journals favor narrow methodologies while underweighting practice relevance.
cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021 documented that accounting research
receives significantly less attention from policymakers and the public than
economics, finance, and other disciplines.

Why? The common explanation centers on reviewer /bias/---topic preferences,
methodological prejudice, or personal networks that systematically
disadvantage certain work. Bias undoubtedly exists. But I offer a
complementary explanation grounded in measurement theory: the statistical
properties of peer review---specifically, low inter-rater reliability
combined with multi-dimensional conjunctive evaluation
criteria---produce systematic dysfunction that disadvantages quality
research even in the /absence/ of bias.

The possibility that chance plays a substantial role in peer review is not
new. cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981 famously demonstrated that "whether or
not a proposal is funded depends in a large proportion of cases upon which
reviewers happen to be selected for it." What is new is formalizing this
intuition and showing that high false rejection rates emerge naturally from
subjectivity alone---no bias required. Using a simulation calibrated to
empirical reliability data, I show that at realistic selectivity levels,
approximately 45% of truly deserving papers are rejected due to measurement
error. The field wisdom about coin-flip odds is not cynicism. It is
arithmetic. And the consequences run deeper than frustration: this error
structure creates perverse incentives in which researchers maximize expected
publications by producing multiple "very good" papers rather than single
"excellent" papers, explaining the prevalence of incremental research and
fragmentation strategies documented in the literature.

The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. Section [[#lit-review]]
reviews prior research on peer review reliability and positions my
contribution. Section [[#model]] develops a formal model of peer review as
noisy signal extraction. Section [[#simulation]] describes my simulation
design and calibration to empirical data. Section [[#results]] presents
results on false rejection rates, researcher incentives, and interaction
effects. Section [[#robustness]] examines robustness to modeling assumptions
and parameter uncertainty. Section [[#discussion]] discusses implications for
accounting journals and possible reforms. Section [[#conclusion]] concludes.

** Inter-Rater Reliability in Peer Review

The empirical foundation for my simulation is a large literature documenting
low inter-rater reliability in peer review across disciplines.
cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 conducted a
meta-analysis of 70 reliability coefficients from 48 studies covering 19,443
manuscripts. The headline finding: a mean intraclass correlation coefficient
(ICC) of 0.34 and a mean Cohen's kappa of 0.17. Only about one-third of the
variance in reviewer assessments reflects true quality differences. The
remaining two-thirds is measurement error.

Critically, this low reliability was consistent across /all/ disciplines
studied, including economics, law, natural sciences, medical sciences, and
social sciences. Low reliability is not a problem specific to any field; it
is an inherent feature of subjective quality assessment.
cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 also found that
multi-dimensional rating systems were associated with /lower/ inter-rater
reliability---a finding with important implications for the AND-gate effect
I model below.

The phenomenon of chance in peer review has been recognized for decades.
cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981 studied 150 NSF proposals, each reviewed
by two independent panels, and famously concluded that "whether or not a
proposal is funded depends in a large proportion of cases upon which
reviewers happen to be selected for it." The same proposal, funded or
rejected, depending on who reads it. This remains one of the most cited
demonstrations of the stochastic nature of peer review.

** Simulation Studies of Peer Review

My work builds on a growing literature using simulation to study peer
review. cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006 developed a probability theory model and
characterized peer review as a "game of chance," finding that 25--50% of
unsuitable papers may be published even with rigorous review. They also
showed that resubmission strategies---submitting rejected papers to other
journals---significantly boost publication chances, a precursor to the
strategic behavior I analyze.

cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017 simulated editors, reviewers, and the
publication process in political science. His key finding: papers at the
80th percentile of quality face essentially coin-flip odds of acceptance.
This is remarkably consistent with my false negative rates under realistic
parameters. Esarey also demonstrated that conjunctive
thresholds---requiring papers to pass on multiple dimensions---amplify error
rates. I extend this insight by formally modeling the AND-gate effect and
calibrating to empirical reliability data.

** Scholarly Consensus and Methodological Agreement

An important theoretical question is whether reviewer disagreement stems
from fundamental uncertainty about quality or from lack of consensus on
evaluation criteria. cite:hargensScholarlyConsensusJournal1988 argued that
interdisciplinary variation in rejection rates reflects variation in
/scholarly consensus/: "When scholars do not share conceptions of
appropriate research problems, theoretical approaches, or research
techniques, they tend to view each other's work as deficient and unworthy of
publication."

This insight matters for accounting, where multiple methodological
traditions coexist (archival, experimental, analytical, qualitative) and
where cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 document widespread
concern that top journals favor narrow methodologies. If reviewers from
different methodological traditions disagree about what constitutes good
research, the effective ICC is lower than the cross-disciplinary average,
amplifying every problem I document.

** The Accounting Publication Context

Several studies document growing dissatisfaction with the accounting
publication process. cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016 compared
publication processes across accounting, economics, finance, management,
marketing, psychology, and natural sciences. Accounting academics perceived
very low citation patterns relative to other disciplines, believed the
review process was deteriorating, and desired acceptance rates roughly twice
as high as current levels.

cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 updated this survey and found
perceptions have worsened since 2015. The rising generation of scholars
holds more negative views than their predecessors. Top journals favor
certain topic areas and methodologies; reviewers underweight practice
relevance; reviewers overweight incremental contribution and methodological
rigor. New assistant professors hold these views /more strongly/ than senior
faculty. The problem is intensifying, not ameliorating.

The consequences extend beyond individual careers.
cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021 documented that accounting research
receives significantly less attention from policymakers and the general
public than economics, finance, and other disciplines. If peer review
dysfunction steers accounting research toward narrow, incremental
contributions, this would help explain the field's limited external impact.

** The Research-Practice Gap

Concerns about accounting research relevance have deep roots.
cite:johnsonRelevanceLostRise1991 argued that management accounting had lost
its relevance to practitioners, becoming divorced from the information needs
of actual organizations. cite:kaplanAccountingScholarshipThat2011 called for
"accounting scholarship that advances professional knowledge and practice,"
arguing that the field had drifted toward technical exercises that fail to
inform either practitioners or policymakers.

More recent work quantifies the gap. cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021
assessed accounting research against three criteria: products and processes
produced, overlap with issues that concern CFOs and CEOs, and whether
academic know-how exceeds that of practitioners. His conclusion: academic
research has strayed from producing work useful to either practitioners or
policymakers, driven by the pressure to publish in elite journals.
cite:fraserAbundantPublicationsMinuscule2020 surveyed accounting
practitioners and found that academic research is largely ignored by the
profession---"abundant publications but minuscule impact."

cite:burtonRelevanceAccountingResearch2022 developed ROAR (Relevance of
Accounting Research) scores by having accounting professionals rate the
titles and abstracts of published papers. The results confirmed what many
suspected: much of what gets published in top journals is perceived as
irrelevant by those who might apply it. Combined with the attention data
from cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021, these findings point to a systematic
disconnect between what peer review rewards and what creates value for the
profession.

** Doctoral Socialization and Methodological Narrowing

A potential mechanism linking peer review dysfunction to research outcomes
is doctoral socialization. cite:fogartyHandThatRocks2010 examined the
American Accounting Association's Doctoral Consortium and found that "the
most important lesson about academic accounting may have been the
righteousness of the field's stratification hierarchy. In other words,
learning one's place in the US academy may be more important than any theory
or technique within academic accounting."

Socialization into hierarchy rather than substance helps explain why narrow
methodologies persist despite widespread dissatisfaction. Doctoral students
learn that success requires conforming to what elite journals accept. Noisy
peer review makes innovative work risky. Rational career management favors
incremental extensions of established approaches. The system perpetuates
itself: new faculty adopt safe strategies, become reviewers who favor
similar work, and train the next generation to do the same.

** Institutional Context: Accounting Journal Review Practices

Understanding the institutional structure of accounting journal review is
essential for calibrating my simulation and interpreting its implications.
The top accounting journals---The Accounting Review (TAR), Journal of
Accounting Research (JAR), and Journal of Accounting and Economics
(JAE)---differ meaningfully in their review processes, and all differ from
journals in other disciplines.

A striking feature of accounting is the small number of reviewers. TAR
typically uses two reviewers per submission. JAR and JAE often use just one.
In the natural sciences, three or more is common; in the medical sciences,
three to five. And yet cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016 documented
that accounting journals have /longer/ times to publication than comparable
journals in economics, finance, management, and psychology---despite using
fewer reviewers.

The single-reviewer model at JAR and JAE is particularly consequential for
my analysis. With one reviewer, there is no averaging of assessments to
reduce noise---one person's noisy signal determines the paper's fate. JAR
and JAE incentivize fast turnaround by paying referees for reviews completed
within four weeks. This improves speed. It does nothing for reliability. The
result is a system optimized for efficiency rather than accuracy.

Desk rejection practices also vary across journals. TAR desk rejects
approximately 5% of submissions, while JAR's desk rejection rate is
approximately 15%. Papers that survive desk rejection face the full review
process, but the low desk rejection rates mean that reviewer resources are
spread across a large pool of submissions. JAR charges substantial
submission fees (currently \$750 for authors from top-tier institutions),
which may deter some submissions but does not address the fundamental
reliability problem.

The institutional structure of accounting review creates conditions where
low inter-rater reliability has maximum impact. One or two reviewers means
limited opportunity for noise to average out. High submission volumes and
pressure for fast turnaround means limited opportunity for careful
deliberation. The simulation results I present below should be interpreted
against this institutional backdrop: accounting journals operate with fewer
reviewers than most disciplines, making them particularly vulnerable to the
measurement error problems I document.

** Interventions and Training

Can peer review be improved through training or procedural reforms?
cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023 conducted a systematic review
of ten randomized controlled trials examining reviewer training
interventions. Training produces "little or no improvement in peer review
quality." Structured review forms similarly fail to improve agreement. Low
reliability is not a problem of reviewer skill or effort. It reflects
fundamental uncertainty in quality assessment.

The Spearman-Brown prophecy formula suggests that reliability improves with
additional reviewers, but with sharply diminishing returns: going from one
to two reviewers halves the error variance, but going from four to five
reduces it by only 20%. My simulation quantifies these diminishing returns
and shows that meaningful improvements require structural changes rather
than marginal additions.

** Contributions

My work makes several contributions.

Prior work established that peer review reliability is low
(cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010), that chance plays a
substantial role in funding decisions (cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981), and
that simulated peer review produces high error rates
(cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017; cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006). I build on this
foundation in several ways.

First, I provide an /integrated/ framework that combines empirical reliability
calibration, multi-dimensional evaluation, and conjunctive decision rules in a
single model. Unlike cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017, who simulates
single-dimension review, I explicitly model the AND-gate structure of
multi-criterion evaluation and show that it substantially amplifies error
rates. This distinction matters because real peer review almost universally
relies on conjunctive, rubric-based decisions---a paper must pass on interest
/and/ rigor /and/ contribution, not merely score well on average.

Second, I derive /incentive implications/ from the error structure. While prior
work documents that peer review is noisy, I show that the specific form of
noise---conjunctive thresholds with low reliability---creates incentives
favoring quantity over quality. This provides a structural explanation for
observed behaviors (salami-slicing, fragmentation) that does not require
assuming researcher irrationality or reviewer bias.

Third, I examine /interaction effects/ (reviewers × noise, reviewers ×
dimensions, threshold × noise) that reveal when interventions are most
effective. These interactions provide actionable guidance for journal design.

Finally, I calibrate to /accounting-specific institutional features/ that
amplify these problems relative to other fields. Accounting journals typically
use only 1--2 reviewers (compared to 3--5 in natural sciences), employ
conjunctive rubrics across multiple criteria, and maintain high selectivity.
This combination is particularly damaging: few reviewers means noise does not
average out, conjunctive rubrics create multiple failure points, and high
selectivity concentrates decisions in the region where noise matters most. The
result is that accounting's institutional structure may produce higher false
rejection rates than fields with more reviewers or compensatory decision rules,
even at identical underlying reliability.

** Setup

I model peer review as a noisy signal extraction problem. A paper $i$ has
true quality on $D$ dimensions:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}_i = (q_{i1}, q_{i2}, \ldots, q_{iD})
\end{equation}
where each dimension might represent interest, rigor, contribution, or other
evaluation criteria. I assume true quality on each dimension is drawn from
a normal distribution:
\begin{equation}
q_{id} \sim N(\mu, \tau^2)
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is mean quality in the submission pool and $\tau^2$ is the
variance of true quality across papers.

** Reviewer Observation

Reviewer $j$ observes paper $i$'s quality on dimension $d$ with measurement
error:
\begin{equation}
\hat{q}_{ijd} = q_{id} + \epsilon_{ijd}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{ijd} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ is independent noise. This noise
captures the inherent subjectivity of quality assessment---reasonable
reviewers can disagree about the same paper even without bias.

When $n$ reviewers evaluate a paper, the editor aggregates their assessments
by averaging:
\begin{equation}
\bar{q}_{id} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\hat{q}_{ijd} = q_{id} + \bar{\epsilon}_{id}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\epsilon}_{id} \sim N(0, \sigma^2/n)$. Averaging reduces noise
variance by a factor of $n$, but with diminishing returns: going from 1 to 2
reviewers halves the variance, but going from 4 to 5 reduces it by only 20%.

** Calibration to Empirical Reliability

The noise parameter $\sigma$ can be calibrated to empirical inter-rater
reliability data. The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) is defined
as:
\begin{equation}
ICC = \frac{\tau^2}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2}
\end{equation}
This represents the proportion of observed variance attributable to true
differences between papers rather than measurement error.

cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 conducted a
meta-analysis of 70 reliability coefficients from 48 studies covering 19,443
manuscripts across disciplines. They found a mean ICC of 0.34, indicating
that only about one-third of the variance in reviewer assessments reflects
true quality differences---the remaining two-thirds is measurement error.
Critically, this low reliability was consistent across /all/ disciplines
studied, including economics, natural sciences, medical sciences, and social
sciences.

With ICC = 0.34 and assuming $\tau = 20$ (standard deviation of true quality
in the submission pool), I can solve for the noise parameter:
\begin{equation}
0.34 = \frac{400}{400 + \sigma^2} \implies \sigma^2 \approx 776 \implies \sigma \approx 28
\end{equation}
I use $\sigma = 30$ in my simulation, which is slightly conservative
(implying marginally more noise than the point estimate) and simplifies
interpretation.

** The AND-Gate Acceptance Rule

Journals typically require papers to meet standards on /multiple/
dimensions. I model this as a conjunctive (AND-gate) decision rule: a paper
is accepted if and only if its observed quality exceeds the threshold $T$ on
/all/ dimensions:
\begin{equation}
Accept_i = \mathbf{1}\left[\bar{q}_{id} \geq T \text{ for all } d \in \{1,\ldots,D\}\right]
\end{equation}

This AND-gate structure is what damages good papers. Consider a paper with
true quality $q_{id} = T + \delta$ on all dimensions---a paper that
genuinely deserves acceptance by margin $\delta$. The probability that
observed quality falls below the threshold on any single dimension is:
\begin{equation}
P(\bar{q}_{id} < T) = \Phi\left(\frac{-\delta}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF.

With AND-gating across $D$ independent dimensions, the probability of
acceptance becomes:
\begin{equation}
P(Accept) = \left[1 - \Phi\left(\frac{-\delta}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^D
\end{equation}

Even if the probability of passing on each dimension is high, the joint
probability of passing on /all/ dimensions can be substantially lower.
Noise on /any/ dimension can sink a deserving paper.

cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 found that
multi-dimensional rating systems are associated with /lower/ inter-rater
reliability, consistent with this theoretical prediction. More dimensions
mean more opportunities for noise to cause rejection.

** Correct Decision and Error Types

I define a paper as truly deserving acceptance if its true quality exceeds
the threshold on all dimensions:
\begin{equation}
ShouldAccept_i = \mathbf{1}\left[q_{id} \geq T \text{ for all } d \in \{1,\ldots,D\}\right]
\end{equation}

This yields four possible outcomes:
- *True Positive (TP):* Paper should be accepted and is accepted
- *False Positive (FP):* Paper should be rejected but is accepted
- *True Negative (TN):* Paper should be rejected and is rejected
- *False Negative (FN):* Paper should be accepted but is rejected

I focus particularly on the false negative rate (FNR):
\begin{equation}
FNR = \frac{FN}{FN + TP}
\end{equation}
This represents the proportion of deserving papers that are incorrectly
rejected---the rate at which good work is filtered out by noise rather than
quality.

** Why Simulation?

Before describing the simulation, it is worth explaining why simulation is
necessary rather than simply analyzing observational data on peer review
outcomes.

The fundamental challenge is that /true quality is unobserved/. Empirical
data can reveal what was accepted and rejected, but not what /should/ have
been accepted and rejected. Without knowing true quality, we cannot
calculate false positive or false negative rates---only acceptance rates.
Journals do not randomly assign reviewers, vary the number of reviewers
experimentally, or publish counterfactual decisions. Causal identification
of peer review mechanics is essentially impossible with observational data.

Simulation sidesteps this problem. Generate papers with /known/ true
quality, subject them to modeled review processes, and compare decisions to
ground truth. The cost is that results depend on modeling assumptions; the
benefit is that we can isolate mechanisms and examine counterfactuals that
could never be observed empirically. My approach calibrates key parameters
(especially noise) to empirical reliability data, grounding the simulation
in observed reviewer behavior while enabling analysis that observational
data cannot support.

** Implementation

I simulate the model described in Section [[#model]]. For each experimental condition, I generate 1,000 papers with
true quality drawn independently on each dimension from $N(50, 20^2)$.
Reviewers observe each dimension with independent noise drawn from $N(0,
\sigma^2)$, and observations are averaged across reviewers. The editor
applies the AND-gate acceptance rule with threshold $T$.

I vary the following parameters:
- *Number of reviewers ($n$):* 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 10
- *Reviewer noise ($\sigma$):* 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40 (baseline 30; interaction experiments use 15, 25, 35)
- *Number of dimensions ($D$):* 2, 3, 4
- *Threshold ($T$):* 40, 50, 60, 70

For each parameter combination, I run 50 independent replications (each
generating 1,000 papers) and report averaged results for stability.
Experiments that estimate acceptance probabilities at specific quality levels
use 10,000 Monte Carlo trials per condition.

The simulation code is implemented in Clojure and is publicly available at
https://github.com/drdebit/publication-sim for replication and extension.

** Baseline Parameters

My baseline parameters are:
- $n = 2$ reviewers (typical for accounting journals)
- $\sigma = 30$ (calibrated to ICC = 0.34)
- $D = 2$ dimensions (interest and rigor)
- $T = 50$ (moderate selectivity)

I then vary each parameter to examine main effects and interactions.

** Design Choices and Assumptions

Several modeling choices warrant explicit justification.

/Dimensionality./ I use 2--4 evaluation dimensions to capture the range of
journal practices. Two dimensions---typically "interest/contribution" and
"execution/rigor"---represent the minimal evaluation structure common to most
journals. Three and four dimensions capture more elaborate rubrics that
additionally separate theoretical contribution, empirical execution, and
writing quality. Equal weighting across dimensions is a simplifying assumption;
in practice, editors may weight dimensions differently. However, equal
weighting is conservative: if one dimension receives low weight but still
operates as a binding constraint (a paper cannot be accepted with fatal
execution flaws regardless of contribution), the AND-gate effect would be
amplified rather than attenuated.

/Symmetric thresholds./ I apply the same threshold to all dimensions. This
simplifies analysis and reflects the intuition that journals have minimum
standards on each criterion. Asymmetric thresholds would change the relative
frequency of rejection reasons but not the fundamental AND-gate dynamic.

/Independence across dimensions./ I assume true quality is independent across
dimensions. In reality, quality may be positively correlated (good researchers
produce papers that are strong on multiple dimensions). Positive correlation
would reduce the AND-gate effect by making it less likely that a paper is
strong on one dimension but weak on another. Simulation with modest positive
correlation ($\rho_{dim} = 0.4$) shows that baseline FNR falls from
approximately 45% to approximately 42%---attenuated but still substantial. My
independence assumption is thus conservative---it produces higher false
rejection rates than would obtain under positive correlation.

/Independence of reviewer errors./ My baseline assumes reviewer errors are
independent, which I relax in the robustness analysis. As shown in Section
[[#robustness]], correlated errors can substantially attenuate the benefit of
additional reviewers.

** Main Finding: False Rejection Rates

|         1 | 51.9%  |  17.6% |    73.8% |       55.8% |
|         2 | 45.0%  |  15.0% |    77.4% |       68.5% |
|         3 | 38.8%  |  13.2% |    80.5% |       75.3% |
|         4 | 37.6%  |  12.4% |    81.4% |       80.2% |

|        10 | 27.7%  |   9.3% |    86.3% |       90.2% |

Under realistic conditions, approximately 45% of papers that deserve
acceptance are rejected due to measurement error alone. With 2 reviewers
(the accounting norm), FNR = 0.45. Nearly half of good papers are filtered
out by noise, not quality.

The effect of reviewer count exhibits clear diminishing returns. Going from
1 to 2 reviewers reduces FNR by 6.9 percentage points---the largest single
improvement. Going from 2 to 5 provides another 10.5 percentage points.
Beyond 5, gains are marginal.

This is consistent with cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017, who found that
papers at the 80th percentile of quality face essentially coin-flip odds
of acceptance. My simulation provides the mechanism: low inter-rater
reliability combined with multi-dimensional evaluation.

The single-reviewer case (n = 1) deserves particular attention because JAR
and JAE frequently use only one reviewer. With a single reviewer, FNR rises
to 52%. More than half of deserving papers are rejected based on one
person's noisy assessment. Adding a second reviewer---the largest marginal
gain in the table---reduces FNR by 6.9 percentage points. Yet these
journals have chosen speed over accuracy. At identical true quality, a
paper's fate at a single-reviewer journal is substantially more random than
at a two-reviewer journal.

** The AND-Gate Effect: Dimensions Amplify Errors

#+CAPTION: Effect of Evaluation Dimensions (AND-gating)
| Dimensions | FNR    | FPR   | Accuracy |
|------------+--------+-------+----------|
|          2 | 45.3%  | 15.3% |    77.1% |
|          3 | 60.0%  |  8.5% |    85.1% |
|          4 | 70.5%  |  4.5% |    91.6% |

Adding evaluation dimensions dramatically increases false rejection rates.
Two dimensions: 45% FNR. Three: 60%. Four: 71%.

Each additional dimension is another opportunity for noise to cause
rejection. A paper must clear the threshold on /every/ dimension, so noise
on /any/ dimension can sink it. The AND-gate is particularly punishing for
borderline-good papers---exactly the papers that selective journals are
trying to distinguish among.

An important finding for top accounting journals: the more selective the

The reason is straightforward. Selective journals are making decisions among
papers clustered near the high end of quality. Most truly deserving papers
are only marginally above threshold, making them vulnerable to downward
noise. At lenient journals, there is more separation between deserving and
undeserving papers, so noise is less likely to cause misclassification.
Selectivity and accuracy work against each other.

** Noise Helps Bad Papers, Hurts Good Papers

#+CAPTION: The lines cross at the threshold: noise systematically redistributes acceptance from deserving to undeserving papers.
#+NAME: fig:quality-noise
#+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.85\textwidth :placement [htbp]
[[file:figures/quality-x-noise.pdf]]

Figure [[fig:quality-noise]] reveals a striking pattern: noise does not
merely add randomness---it systematically redistributes acceptance
probability from good papers to bad ones. At quality = 90, acceptance
probability drops from 94% with low noise to 52% with high noise. At
quality = 50, acceptance probability /rises/ from 0.2% to 7%. Noise takes
from the deserving and gives to the undeserving.

The lines converge near the threshold (quality = 70), where acceptance
probability is roughly 25% regardless of noise level. Borderline papers
face a coin-flip regardless of system reliability. The benefit of writing
excellent work is dramatically eroded by noise.

*** The Threshold as Crossover Point

To understand this redistribution more precisely, I examine how the effect of
noise varies across the full quality spectrum.

#+CAPTION: Noise effect by quality level: positive delta indicates noise helps
| Quality | P(Low Noise) | P(High Noise) |  Delta | Effect  |
|---------+--------------+---------------+--------+---------|
|      50 |         0.1% |          6.9% |  +6.8% | Helps   |
|      58 |         1.6% |         12.7% | +11.0% | Helps   |
|      64 |         7.0% |         18.3% | +11.2% | Helps   |
|      70 |        24.7% |         25.2% |  +0.5% | Neutral |
|      76 |        50.4% |         33.6% | -16.8% | Hurts   |
|      84 |        81.1% |         45.5% | -35.6% | Hurts   |
|      90 |        94.4% |         53.3% | -41.1% | Hurts   |

The threshold acts as a precise crossover point. Below threshold, noise
provides "lottery tickets"---random chances to be pushed above the acceptance
bar. The maximum benefit occurs around 6--12 points below threshold (quality
58--64), where high noise increases acceptance probability by approximately 11
percentage points. Above threshold, noise is purely destructive, with the
damage increasing with quality: excellent papers (quality 90) lose over 40
percentage points of acceptance probability when noise is high.

This asymmetry has a simple interpretation: noise creates variance around true
quality. For papers below threshold, upward variance helps (acceptance) while
downward variance doesn't matter (rejection either way). For papers above
threshold, downward variance hurts (rejection) while upward variance doesn't
matter (acceptance either way). The net effect is redistribution from good to
mediocre.

*** Convergence to Mediocrity

This redistribution has a profound implication: noise pulls the /published/
literature toward mediocrity from both ends of the quality distribution.

Noise does not change true quality---it changes /observed/ quality. Some
excellent papers are observed as merely good and rejected. Some mediocre
papers are observed as acceptable and published. The threshold acts as an
attractor: with sufficient noise, acceptance rates across quality levels
converge toward whatever baseline rate exists at the threshold.

Consider the compression under high noise:
- Quality 90 → 53% acceptance (down from 94% with low noise)
- Quality 50 → 7% acceptance (up from 0.1% with low noise)

The ratio of acceptance rates shrinks from 940:1 to 8:1. High noise
dramatically weakens the correlation between true quality and publication.
The published literature becomes less representative of actual quality
differences and more representative of luck.

An observer examining only published papers would see a narrower quality
distribution than actually exists in submissions---the tails are truncated
from both ends. Excellent papers that deserved publication are missing.
Mediocre papers that should have been filtered out are present. The
published record /systematically underrepresents/ the true variance in
research quality.

This convergence to mediocrity helps explain why top journals are perceived
as publishing increasingly similar work. Noise compresses the quality
distribution of accepted papers. Reviewers from similar methodological
backgrounds add correlated noise (see Section [[#robustness]]). The
published literature trends toward a narrow band of "safe" research that
reliably clears the threshold without attempting excellence---exactly the
pattern documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024.

This mechanism operates independently of editorial intent or reviewer
competence. A journal staffed entirely by well-meaning, highly-qualified
reviewers will still exhibit convergence to mediocrity if the review
process is sufficiently noisy. The problem is structural, not personnel.

** Quality vs. Volume: Incentive Implications

The noisy structure of peer review creates incentives that favor quantity
over quality. To see why, compare expected publications under four
hypothetical strategies for a researcher with fixed total effort (at
threshold = 70, 2 reviewers, noise SD = 30):

| Two good       |      2 |      72 |     28.5% |            0.57 |
| One excellent  |      1 |      85 |     56.4% |            0.56 |

The "two very good" strategy yields 46% higher expected publications than

*** Does the Lottery Ticket Strategy Work?

If noise helps below-threshold papers, could a researcher maximize expected
publications by producing /many/ mediocre papers and treating each as a
lottery ticket? I extend the strategy comparison to include below-threshold
quality levels.

#+CAPTION: Extended Strategy Comparison (including below-threshold papers)
| Strategy         | Papers | Quality | P(Accept) | E[Pubs] | vs. Threshold |
|------------------+--------+---------+-----------+---------+---------------|
| Two very good    |      2 |      80 |     46.0% |    0.92 | Above         |
| Two good         |      2 |      75 |     35.4% |    0.71 | Above         |
| One excellent    |      1 |      90 |     67.6% |    0.68 | Above         |
| Four decent      |      4 |      65 |     16.6% |    0.66 | Below         |

| Eight poor       |      8 |      45 |      1.4% |    0.11 | Below         |
| Sixteen lottery  |     16 |      35 |      0.3% |    0.04 | Below         |

The lottery ticket strategy fails. Acceptance probability drops faster than
paper count increases. Sixteen papers at quality 35 yield only 0.04
expected publications, far below one excellent paper (0.68).

But /slightly/ below-threshold papers can compete with excellent ones. Four
papers at quality 65 (five points below threshold) yield 0.66 expected
publications---within 3% of one excellent paper at quality 90. There
is a "near-miss" region where the lottery effect is meaningful but not
dominant.

The optimal strategy remains firmly above threshold: "two very good" papers
at quality 80 dominate all alternatives by a wide margin (0.92 expected
publications). The key insight is that researchers should aim for the
/lowest quality that reliably clears the threshold/, not the highest quality
possible. Excess quality above threshold provides diminishing returns.
Quality below threshold wastes effort on long-shot lottery tickets.

*** Implications for Researcher Behavior

If researchers respond rationally to these incentives---and there is every
reason to think they do---this helps explain several observed phenomena:
- The prevalence of "salami-slicing" (fragmenting research into minimal
  publishable units)
- Field wisdom to "write twice as many papers as you think you need"
- The trend toward more incremental contributions noted by
  cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024
- The observation that most papers aim for "good enough" rather than
  "exceptional"

A methodological note: I do not model researcher behavior directly. The
simulation calculates acceptance probabilities at different quality levels,
not equilibrium strategies that account for effort costs, career dynamics,
co-authorship, or multi-period optimization. The strategy comparison is
/comparative statics/---how expected publications vary with quality and
quantity choices---not predictions about actual behavior. A full equilibrium
model would require additional assumptions beyond this paper's scope.

That said, the incentive gradients are consistent with observed behavior and
with cite:azoulayDoesPeerReview2025, who provide evidence that peer review
penalizes researchers who pursue bold, unconventional work. If innovative
research faces higher variance in reviewer assessments---less consensus on
what "good" looks like---the AND-gate effect is amplified, making
conservative, incremental work relatively more attractive in expected value
terms.

* Robustness and Sensitivity Analysis

:CUSTOM_ID: robustness
:END:

My main results depend on modeling assumptions. This section examines
sensitivity to key parameters and relaxes assumptions that may be
unrealistic. Specific magnitudes vary, but the qualitative conclusions---high
false rejection rates, perverse incentives, and diminishing returns to
reviewers---are robust across specifications.

** Sensitivity to Inter-Rater Reliability Calibration

My baseline calibration uses ICC = 0.34 from
cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010. However, this estimate
comes from a meta-analysis spanning disciplines, and the true ICC for
accounting may differ. I examine sensitivity across a range of plausible
values.

| ICC  | Implied σ | FNR    | Accuracy |
|------+-----------+--------+----------|
| 0.20 |      40.0 | 52.1%  |    74.2% |
| 0.25 |      34.6 | 49.3%  |    75.6% |
| 0.30 |      30.6 | 46.5%  |    76.9% |
| 0.34 |      27.9 | 43.6%  |    78.1% |

| 0.45 |      22.1 | 37.6%  |    81.3% |

FNR ranges from 35.5% (ICC = 0.50) to 52.1% (ICC = 0.20). Even at the
most optimistic reliability level, more than one-third of deserving papers
are rejected. At lower reliability levels---which likely characterize
evaluations of innovative or cross-disciplinary work---the majority of
deserving papers face rejection.

This range brackets accounting-specific scenarios. If accounting reviews
involve less consensus than the cross-disciplinary average (competing
methodological traditions suggest they might), effective ICC falls below
0.34. If accounting has developed clearer quality criteria than other
fields, ICC might be higher. The qualitative conclusions---substantial false
rejection and perverse incentives---hold throughout this range.

** Aggregation Method: Mean vs. Median

My baseline model assumes editors aggregate reviewer scores by averaging. An
alternative is median aggregation, which is more robust to outlier reviewers.

#+CAPTION: Mean vs. Median Aggregation
| Reviewers | Method | Accuracy | FNR    |
|-----------+--------+----------+--------|
|         2 | Mean   |    77.9% | 43.9%  |

Mean aggregation slightly outperforms median across reviewer counts. With 5
reviewers, mean achieves 82.8% accuracy versus 80.7% for median---a difference
of 2.1 percentage points. This occurs because my model assumes normally
distributed noise without outliers, where averaging is statistically optimal.
In practice, if some reviewers provide "adversarial" reviews (extremely
negative assessments unrelated to quality), median aggregation could
outperform; my model does not capture this phenomenon.

** Correlated Reviewer Errors

My baseline assumes reviewer errors are independent---one reviewer's
assessment does not influence another's. This assumption may be violated if:
(1) reviewers share methodological training that biases them similarly; (2)
reviewers observe each other's assessments in sequential review; or (3)
reviewers draw on common information (e.g., the same literature) that
systematically misleads them.

I model correlated errors by decomposing each reviewer's noise into a shared
component and an independent component: $\epsilon_{ijd} = \sqrt{\rho}\,\eta_{id} + \sqrt{1-\rho}\,\nu_{ijd}$,
where $\eta_{id}$ is shared across reviewers and $\nu_{ijd}$ is independent.
This produces equicorrelated noise with pairwise correlation $\rho$ between reviewers.
Note that this captures correlation /across reviewers/---the tendency for
different reviewers to err in the same direction on the same paper---rather
than correlation /within a reviewer across dimensions/ (e.g., a harsh reviewer
being harsh on all criteria). The latter would affect how dimensions combine
but not how reviewers aggregate; the former directly undermines the benefit of
multiple reviewers.

#+CAPTION: Effect of Correlated Reviewer Errors
| Correlation | Reviewers | Accuracy | FNR    |
|-------------+-----------+----------+--------|
|        0.00 |         2 |    77.9% | 43.9%  |
|        0.00 |         3 |    80.1% | 39.9%  |
|        0.00 |         5 |    83.4% | 33.9%  |
|        0.25 |         2 |    76.9% | 46.3%  |
|        0.25 |         3 |    78.5% | 43.2%  |
|        0.25 |         5 |    80.2% | 39.9%  |
|        0.50 |         2 |    75.0% | 49.9%  |
|        0.50 |         3 |    76.0% | 49.0%  |
|        0.50 |         5 |    76.6% | 47.3%  |
|        0.75 |         2 |    75.2% | 50.5%  |
|        0.75 |         3 |    75.5% | 49.7%  |
|        0.75 |         5 |    75.5% | 49.7%  |

Correlated errors eliminate the benefit of additional reviewers. At $\rho =
0$ (independent errors), going from 2 to 5 reviewers improves accuracy from
77.9% to 83.4%---a gain of 5.5 percentage points. At $\rho = 0.75$ (highly
correlated errors), the same change yields essentially nothing (75.2% to
75.5%).

Correlated errors do not average out. If all reviewers share a
methodological worldview that disadvantages certain research approaches,
adding more reviewers from the same pool merely confirms the shared bias.

But the empirical evidence from accounting suggests that reviewer
/disagreement/, not agreement, is the dominant pattern. If reviewers shared
systematic biases (high correlation), we would expect quick decisions: both
reviewers agree, the process concludes rapidly. Instead, we observe the
opposite. Accounting journals have longer times to publication than
comparable journals in other fields despite using fewer reviewers
(cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016). Papers routinely go through
multiple revision rounds as authors attempt to satisfy divergent reviewer
demands. Management Science imposed an explicit two-round limit to prevent
excessive iterations---a policy unnecessary if reviewers typically agreed.
Papers rejected at one journal are often accepted at another with different
reviewers, suggesting the original rejection reflected reviewer-specific
views rather than consensus about quality.

This pattern is consistent with independent errors. The low ICC reported by
cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 is not measurement
error in the traditional sense---noise that could be reduced with better
instruments. It reflects genuine lack of consensus about what constitutes
quality. Reviewers hold fundamentally different views about what makes a
paper good, leading to the contradictory demands and extended review
processes that characterize the field.

The correlated-errors analysis should therefore be interpreted as a
/counterfactual/: what would happen if reviewers /did/ agree? Adding
reviewers would provide little benefit---but decisions would be faster and
more consistent. The fact that we observe slow, inconsistent decisions
suggests the independent-errors baseline is the empirically relevant case.
My main results are grounded in observed reviewer behavior.

** Sensitivity to Quality Variance

My baseline assumes true quality has standard deviation $\tau = 20$. This
parameter affects both the difficulty of discrimination (smaller $\tau$ means
papers are more similar) and the calibrated noise level (since ICC depends on
both $\tau$ and $\sigma$).

#+CAPTION: Sensitivity to Quality Variance (τ)
| τ  | FNR    | Accuracy |
|----+--------+----------|
| 15 | 52.9%  |    74.0% |
| 20 | 43.6%  |    78.1% |
| 25 | 38.6%  |    80.7% |
| 30 | 33.9%  |    83.1% |

Lower quality variance (τ = 15) increases FNR to 52.9%; higher variance (τ
= 30) reduces it to 33.9%. Two reasons. First, with less variance in true
quality, papers cluster more tightly near the threshold, where noise is
most likely to cause misclassification. Second, at fixed ICC, lower $\tau$
implies lower $\sigma$ (less absolute noise), but the relative noise
compared to quality differences remains constant.

The implication: if accounting submissions exhibit relatively homogeneous
quality---most papers from qualified researchers meeting professional
standards---the effective false rejection rate is higher than my baseline
estimate. If quality is highly variable, discrimination is easier than my
model suggests.

** Theoretical Considerations: Non-Normal Distributions

My model assumes normally distributed true quality and reviewer noise.
Empirical quality distributions may differ: true quality might be right-skewed
(most papers are mediocre, few are excellent) or heavy-tailed (occasional
breakthrough papers).

I do not simulate alternative distributions, but can reason about their
effects. Right-skewed quality distributions would place more papers near the
left tail, where noise is more likely to cause false /positives/ than false
negatives. This could mitigate FNR concerns for the typical paper while
exacerbating FPR concerns. Heavy-tailed noise distributions (e.g., occasional
highly discordant reviewers) would reduce the effectiveness of averaging and
potentially favor median aggregation.

The key insight is that my baseline estimates assume symmetric, well-behaved
distributions. Departures from normality could either amplify or attenuate
specific effects, but the fundamental tension between selectivity and
reliability remains.

** Summary of Robustness Findings

To bound the estimates, I summarize results under optimistic and pessimistic
assumptions:

#+CAPTION: Plausible Bounds on False Negative Rate
| Scenario    | ICC  | τ  | Reviewers | FNR   | Interpretation                         |
|-------------+------+----+-----------+-------+----------------------------------------|
| Optimistic  | 0.50 | 30 |         3 | ~28%  | High reliability, quality variance     |
| Baseline    | 0.34 | 20 |         2 | ~45%  | Calibrated to Bornmann et al.          |
| Pessimistic | 0.20 | 15 |         1 | ~55%  | Low reliability, single reviewer       |
| Accounting  | 0.34 | 20 |         1 | ~52%  | JAR/JAE single-reviewer model          |

Even under optimistic assumptions, more than one-quarter of deserving papers
are rejected. Under the single-reviewer model used by some accounting journals,
the majority of deserving papers face rejection. The baseline estimate of ~45%
should be interpreted as a central estimate within a range of roughly 28--55%
depending on institutional features and true reliability.

Across sensitivity analyses, several findings are robust:

1. *High false rejection rates persist.* Even at the most optimistic ICC (0.50)
   and highest quality variance (τ = 30), FNR exceeds 33%. Under pessimistic
   but plausible assumptions, FNR exceeds 50%.

2. *Diminishing returns to reviewers.* Going from 1 to 2 reviewers provides the
   largest benefit; going beyond 5 provides marginal improvement. This finding
   is robust across ICC values and aggregation methods.

3. *Correlated errors undermine reviewer aggregation.* If reviewers share
   systematic biases, adding reviewers provides little benefit. This suggests
   reviewer diversity may matter more than reviewer quantity.

4. *Perverse incentives persist.* The incentive to produce multiple
   moderate-quality papers rather than single excellent papers emerges from
   stochastic acceptance, which is present regardless of specific parameter
   values.

The qualitative story is robust. The quantitative estimates depend on
parameters that are imperfectly known. Both of these statements should
inform how readers interpret the results.

** Reframing the Problem

My findings reframe the accounting publication "problem." The conventional
view attributes publication difficulties to reviewer bias---topic
preferences, methodological prejudice, favoritism. Bias surely exists. But
high false rejection rates and perverse incentives emerge naturally from
the /structure/ of peer review even without any bias.

This distinction matters for reform. If the problem were primarily bias,
solutions would focus on reviewer training, blind review, or diversifying
reviewer pools. But if the problem is structural noise, those interventions
miss the target. And indeed, cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023
found that training produces "little or no improvement in peer review
quality."

** Implications for Editors and Journals

Several directions follow for editors seeking to improve decision quality.

*Reviewer quantity and diversity.* The largest accuracy gains come from going
from 1 to 2 reviewers, but meaningful improvements continue through 3-5
reviewers. If accounting journals typically use 1-2 reviewers, there is
accuracy left on the table. However, my correlated-errors analysis suggests
that reviewer /diversity/ may matter more than reviewer /quantity/. Adding a
third reviewer from the same methodological tradition as the first two provides
less benefit than adding a reviewer with different training. Editors might
prioritize assembling methodologically diverse review teams over simply
increasing reviewer counts.

*Decision rule architecture.* Comprehensive rubrics with many evaluation
criteria /reduce/ decision quality by creating more opportunities for noise
to reject good papers. Journals should consider compensatory rather than
conjunctive decision rules---allowing excellence on one dimension to offset
weakness on another---or reducing the number of explicit evaluation
dimensions. Requiring papers to "pass" on interest, rigor, contribution,
and writing creates four independent opportunities for noise to sink a
deserving paper.

*Transparent acknowledgment of uncertainty.* Roughly half of rejection
decisions for borderline-good papers are determined by noise rather than
true quality. Journals could acknowledge this in rejection letters,
distinguishing between "clear reject" (paper unlikely to succeed anywhere)
and "unlucky reject" (paper might well have been accepted with different
reviewers). This transparency would reduce author frustration and encourage
appropriate resubmission behavior.

*Structural alternatives.* Given that training doesn't improve reliability,
journals might explore structural changes. Lottery elements for borderline
papers would make randomness explicit rather than hidden. Registered reports
separate evaluation of research questions from evaluation of results,
potentially reducing noise from hindsight bias. Multi-stage review that filters
on different dimensions sequentially rather than conjunctively could improve
accuracy for papers that clear initial screens.

*Experimentation and measurement.* Journals could measure their own reliability
by occasionally having papers reviewed by independent panels, as in
cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981. This would provide field-specific reliability
estimates and allow journals to evaluate whether interventions actually improve
decision quality. Without measurement, improvement is difficult.

*** Summary: Why Common Intuitions Fail

Several of these implications are counterintuitive. Why do common-sense
approaches to improving peer review backfire?

/More detailed rubrics do not help./ Clearer criteria should improve
reliability, right? No. Each additional criterion is another dimension on
which noise can cause rejection. AND-gating turns comprehensive evaluation
into a liability.

/Adding reviewers has sharply diminishing returns./ More opinions should
converge on truth, right? Only at $1/\sqrt{n}$. Going from 2 to 10 reviewers
improves accuracy by about 9 percentage points---often not worth the cost.

/Reviewer training does not improve reliability./ Better training should
produce better reviews, right? Low reliability reflects fundamental
uncertainty about quality, not reviewer incompetence
(cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023).

/Transparency does not automatically improve outcomes./ Open review or
published referee reports should discipline reviewers, right? The problem is
noise, not effort. Reviewers who try harder still disagree.

These failures share a common root: they assume peer review is a solvable
measurement problem rather than an inherently noisy estimation problem. Once
this reframing is accepted, attention shifts from "fixing reviewers" to
"designing systems that are robust to noise."

** Implications for Researchers

My findings also speak directly to researchers navigating the publication
system.

*Calibrating expectations.* The field wisdom that good papers have roughly
50-50 odds at top journals is not cynicism. It is arithmetic. Researchers
should calibrate expectations accordingly, treating rejection as noisy
signal rather than definitive quality judgment. A rejection from one journal
provides limited information about likely success at another with different
reviewers.

*Portfolio strategy.* Researchers maximize expected publications by
producing multiple papers rather than concentrating effort on single
masterworks. This is not advice to produce low-quality work. Conditional on
maintaining quality, distributing effort across multiple projects dominates
concentration. "Two very good papers" outperforms "one excellent paper" by
46% in expected publications.

*Resubmission persistence.* Given high false negative rates, persistence in
resubmission is rational. A paper rejected due to noise at one journal may
succeed at another with different reviewers. cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006 showed
that resubmission strategies significantly boost publication chances. However,
this creates its own inefficiencies: reviewer effort is duplicated, and
persistence rather than quality becomes a determinant of publication.

*Risk and innovation.* Innovative or cross-paradigm research faces higher
variance in reviewer assessments---less consensus on what "good" looks
like---which amplifies the AND-gate effect. Early-career researchers facing
tenure clocks rationally choose incremental extensions of established
approaches over risky innovation. Individually rational; collectively
suboptimal.

*Interpreting feedback.* Researchers should weight reviewer feedback by
considering the reliability of peer review. Detailed, specific critiques likely
contain signal; vague objections to "contribution" or "interest" may reflect
noise or taste differences rather than fundamental problems. The appropriate
response to rejection depends on whether the feedback identifies fixable issues
or merely reveals that reviewers were unpersuaded.

** Implications for the Profession

Beyond individual journals and researchers, my findings raise broader
concerns about knowledge production in accounting.

*Systematic bias against innovation.* Noisy peer review creates incentives
for incremental research. Doctoral socialization reinforces these incentives
(cite:fogartyHandThatRocks2010). The profession systematically underinvests
in innovative work. The research-practice gap documented by
cite:kaplanAccountingScholarshipThat2011 and cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021
reflects, at least in part, a publication system that rewards safe,
incremental contributions over risky attempts to address important problems.

*Talent allocation.* High false rejection rates impose costs beyond
frustration. Talented researchers who experience repeated rejection exit
academia or redirect effort toward consulting, teaching, or administration.
If rejection is partly random, some of those who exit are high-quality
researchers who drew unlucky reviewer assignments. The profession loses
their contributions.

*Credentialing vs. knowledge production.* There is a tension between peer
review's credentialing function (certifying researcher quality for tenure
and promotion) and its knowledge production function (filtering ideas to
advance understanding). A noisy credentialing system may still "work" if
randomness averages out across careers. A noisy knowledge filter fails
directly: good ideas are rejected, resources are wasted on duplicated review
effort, and incremental work crowds out innovation.

*Collective action problems.* Individual journals face little incentive to
invest in reliability improvements. Adding reviewers is costly. Measuring
reliability is effort-intensive. Journals compete on prestige, not decision
accuracy. Improving the system requires coordinated action---from
professional associations, from funders who could require reliability
reporting, or from researchers who could collectively demand better
processes.

*Alternative models.* Other fields have experimented with alternatives:
open review (where reviews are published), post-publication review (where
filtering happens after publication), preprint servers (which separate
dissemination from certification), and overlay journals (which curate rather
than gatekeep). Accounting has been conservative in adopting any of these.
The costs of the traditional model are higher than commonly recognized. The
field should be experimenting.

** Limitations

My simulation makes simplifying assumptions. Reviewer noise is independent
and identically distributed; in reality, reviewers may have correlated
biases. Dimensions are independent; in reality, true quality may be
correlated across dimensions. Aggregation is by simple averaging; in
reality, editors may weight reviewers differently.

These simplifications likely make my estimates /conservative/. Correlated
reviewer errors would not average out as effectively. Positive dimension
correlation would /reduce/ the AND-gate effect (a paper strong on one dimension
is likely strong on others), attenuating false rejection rates. Non-uniform
weighting could help or hurt depending on whether editors correctly identify
reliable reviewers.

I also do not model the full publication ecosystem, including resubmission
to other journals, desk rejection, or strategic author behavior. Future work
could extend the simulation to capture these dynamics.

Finally, I model the editor as mechanically applying a conjunctive threshold,
but real editors exercise discretion. An experienced editor may override a
split decision, weigh one reviewer's expertise more heavily, or recognize when
a negative review reflects taste rather than quality. Such discretion could
partially mitigate the AND-gate effect if editors systematically correct for
noise. However, editorial discretion could also introduce new biases, and the
fundamental reliability problem remains: editors must still interpret noisy
signals. The net effect of discretion is an empirical question that my
simulation does not address.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

I model peer review as noisy signal extraction and use simulation to
examine its implications for false rejection rates and researcher
incentives. Calibrating to empirical inter-rater reliability data (ICC =
0.34), I find that approximately 45% of deserving papers are rejected due to
measurement error alone. Low reliability, multi-dimensional evaluation, and
high selectivity combine to create a system that systematically rejects good
work---not because reviewers are biased, but because the signal-to-noise
ratio is low.

This reframes how to interpret and reform the publication process. The
problem is not bad reviewers. It is a structurally noisy system.
Well-intentioned actors produce high false rejection rates when reliability
is low and standards are high. Recognizing this shifts attention from
reviewer training (which does not work) to structural interventions: more
reviewers, different decision rules, reviewer diversity, and explicit
acknowledgment of uncertainty.

Noise also creates perverse incentives. Researchers maximize expected
publications by producing multiple moderate-quality papers rather than
single excellent papers---"two very good" dominates "one excellent" by 46%
in expected output. This helps explain the prevalence of incremental
research and "salami-slicing" documented in the literature. Observed
research practices are rational responses to structural incentives, not
failures of scholarly values.

** Future Research Directions

Several extensions would strengthen this analysis.

First, accounting-specific reliability data---obtained by having papers
reviewed by independent panels---would allow more precise estimates of
false rejection rates. Such data could also test whether reliability varies
by methodology, topic area, or paper type.

Second, modeling the full publication ecosystem would capture dynamics I
abstract away. Papers rejected at one journal are revised and resubmitted
elsewhere; modeling this process would reveal system-level outcomes
(eventual publication rates, total reviewer effort) rather than
single-journal outcomes.

Third, my model treats quality as exogenous, but researchers choose how to
allocate effort. An equilibrium model could reveal feedback effects I do not
capture. If noisy review induces quantity-over-quality strategies, the
resulting submissions become more homogeneous in quality (lower $\tau$),
which my sensitivity analysis suggests would /increase/ false rejection
rates, further strengthening incentives for quantity over quality. The
system could converge to a low-quality, high-noise equilibrium even if it
began with higher-quality submissions.

Fourth, comparative analysis across fields could test whether accounting's
institutional features---few reviewers, long review times, high rejection
rates---produce worse outcomes than alternative configurations. Natural
experiments, such as journals that changed reviewer counts or decision
processes, could provide causal evidence.

Finally, field experiments would move from diagnosis to treatment. Journals
could randomly assign papers to different numbers of reviewers, different
decision rules, or different reviewer selection procedures, measuring
effects on eventual outcomes (citation, replication, practitioner impact)
rather than just immediate acceptance decisions.

** Broader Implications

My analysis speaks to concerns beyond accounting. Peer review is the
primary quality filter for scholarly knowledge across disciplines, yet its
reliability is consistently low. If the problems I document are
general---and the cross-disciplinary consistency of ICC estimates suggests
they are---then scholarly communication systems worldwide are systematically
rejecting good work and rewarding quantity over quality.

The downstream consequences are serious. Funders, universities, and
governments rely on publication records to allocate resources, grant tenure,
and assess research impact. If publication is substantially random, these
downstream decisions inherit that randomness. A researcher denied tenure due
to insufficient publications may have drawn unlucky reviewers. A field that
appears unproductive may face harsher review standards. Noisy signals used
for high-stakes decisions amplify the costs of low reliability.

More fundamentally: is peer review---in its current form---the right
institution for filtering scholarly knowledge? Peer review emerged in an
era of scarce publication capacity, when physical journals had page limits
and printing costs. In the digital era, these constraints have relaxed, yet
we retain pre-digital filtering mechanisms. Post-publication review, open
access archives, and algorithmic curation offer alternatives that might
better balance quality control against the costs of false rejection.

I do not advocate abandoning peer review, which provides valuable functions
beyond filtering: feedback improves papers, review trains scholars in
critical evaluation, and the process builds scholarly community. But the
costs of traditional peer review are higher than commonly acknowledged, and
experimentation with alternatives deserves serious consideration.

** Closing Thoughts

The frustration documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024
is real. Accounting researchers believe the publication process is broken,
that good work is rejected while narrow work succeeds, and that the system
rewards the wrong behaviors. These perceptions are not paranoia. They
reflect accurate intuitions about the structural properties of noisy
evaluation.

But attributing dysfunction primarily to bias misdirects reform efforts.
Bias is addressable through training, diversification, and vigilance. Noise
is addressable only through structural change. If the profession spends its
reform energy on bias while the real problem is noise, efforts fail and the
profession concludes---incorrectly---that the problem is intractable.

If accounting research is to matter---to practitioners, policymakers, and
the public---the field needs publication systems that reliably identify and
disseminate good work. Currently, we do not have such systems. Building them
is among the most important challenges facing the profession.
