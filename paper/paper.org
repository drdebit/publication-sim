* Abstract

Recent surveys document growing dissatisfaction with the accounting
publication process: researchers believe acceptance rates should nearly
double and that top journals increasingly favor narrow methodologies. The
conventional explanation is reviewer bias. But the deeper problem is
reviewer disagreement---diligent, competent reviewers who genuinely differ
on what constitutes good research. When reviewers disagree and a paper must
pass on every evaluation dimension, disagreement on any single dimension
sinks a deserving paper. Using a simulation calibrated to empirical
inter-rater agreement data (ICC = 0.34), I show that at realistic
selectivity levels, approximately 45% of deserving papers are rejected due
to reviewer disagreement alone. This creates perverse incentives: researchers
maximize expected publications by producing multiple moderate-quality papers
rather than single excellent papers, and methodological conformity emerges as
a rational defense against subjectivity---clustering near established
approaches reduces the scope for disagreement. These patterns are
consistent with a structural mechanism: reviewers disagree because there is
no shared definition of what "good" means, and that distinction points
toward different remedies. 

* Introduction

Accounting scholars widely report that good papers are rejected from top
journals and that acceptance rates are lower than they should be. Evidence
from surveys is consistent with this perception. cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016
find that accounting academics perceive low citation patterns and a
deteriorating review process. cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024
show these perceptions have worsened and that respondents believe acceptance
rates should nearly double, with concerns that top journals favor narrow
methodologies. cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021 document that accounting
research receives less attention from policymakers and the public than
economics and finance.

The common explanation centers on reviewer /bias/---topic preferences,
methodological prejudice, or personal networks that systematically
disadvantage certain work. Bias undoubtedly exists. However, the empirical
patterns in peer review are consistent with a different mechanism: reviewer
disagreement. In markets, disagreement is associated with gradual
information diffusion rather than immediate price adjustment
(cite:fedykDisagreementNewsGradual2021). In accounting, journals have longer
times to publication than comparable journals despite using fewer reviewers
(cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016). Papers routinely go through
multiple revision rounds as authors attempt to satisfy divergent reviewer
demands. Management Science imposed an explicit two-round limit to prevent
excessive iterations, a policy that would be unnecessary if reviewers
typically agreed.

Peer reviewers are diligent, competent professionals who often disagree
about what constitutes good research. There is no shared definition of
"interest," "contribution," or even "rigor" that reliably produces
consensus. The empirical evidence supports this: cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010
find that roughly two-thirds of the variance in reviewer assessments
reflects disagreement rather than true quality differences, and training
interventions produce little or no improvement
(cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023).

When papers must pass on every evaluation dimension---interest /and/ rigor
/and/ contribution---disagreement on any single dimension can sink a
deserving paper. This is the AND-gate structure of peer review. A paper that
is genuinely excellent can be rejected because one reviewer finds the topic
uninteresting while another finds the execution imperfect. Multiply this
across two or three dimensions, and the probability that a good paper
survives unscathed falls sharply.

Using a simulation calibrated to empirical inter-rater agreement data, I
show that this mechanism produces false rejection rates of approximately
45%---nearly half of deserving papers rejected from disagreement alone.
These rates are consistent with the field's coin-flip intuition about
acceptance.

The consequences extend beyond frustration. This structure creates
incentives to maximize expected publications by producing multiple
moderate-quality papers rather than a single excellent paper, and it
encourages methodological conformity. When reviewers disagree about what
"good" looks like for novel work, clustering near established approaches
reduces the scope for disagreement. This pattern is consistent with the
methodological narrowing documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024,
the research-practice gap documented by cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021,
and the doctoral socialization patterns documented by
cite:fogartyHandThatRocks2010.

This paper reframes how we understand publication dysfunction. The problem
is not simply biased reviewers or an incompetent system. It is structural
disagreement operating through a decision architecture that amplifies its
effects. Simulation is the tool; the contribution is the insight.

** Contributions

My work makes several contributions to the accounting profession's
understanding of its own publication process.

First, I /reframe/ the problem. The conventional narrative attributes
publication dysfunction to reviewer bias, editorial gatekeeping, or
insufficient rigor. I show that the patterns the profession
observes---high false rejection rates, incremental research, methodological
narrowing---emerge naturally from structural disagreement alone, without
requiring any bias or incompetence. This reframing matters because it
redirects reform efforts from "fixing reviewers" to redesigning systems
that are robust to disagreement.

Second, I show that /disagreement alone/ produces the profession's observed
dysfunction. Using a simulation calibrated to empirical inter-rater
agreement data, I demonstrate that approximately 45% of deserving papers
are rejected when reviewers disagree at empirically documented levels and
papers must pass on multiple evaluation dimensions. This provides a
structural explanation for observed behaviors---salami-slicing,
fragmentation, incremental extensions---that does not require assuming
researcher irrationality or reviewer bias.

Third, I identify /methodological conformity as a rational but collectively
harmful response/ to disagreement. When reviewers cannot agree on what
"good" looks like for novel work, clustering near established
approaches---where evaluation criteria are better defined and consensus is
higher---reduces rejection risk. This explains the methodological narrowing
documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 and the
research-practice gap documented by cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021.

Fourth, I show /why common reforms fail/ and what structural changes would
help. Training does not work because reviewers are already competent. Detailed
rubrics do not work because they formalize the dimensions on which reviewers
disagree. What does help: fewer evaluation dimensions, compensatory rather than
conjunctive decision rules, and reviewer diversity rather than reviewer
quantity.

The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. Section [[#lit-review]]
reviews prior research on reviewer agreement and positions my
contribution. Section [[#model]] develops a formal model of peer review.
Section [[#simulation]] describes my simulation design and calibration to
empirical data. Section [[#results]] presents results on false rejection

implications for editors, researchers, and the profession. Section
[[#robustness]] examines robustness to modeling assumptions and parameter
uncertainty. Section [[#limitations]] discusses limitations. Section
[[#conclusion]] concludes.

** Inter-Rater Agreement in Peer Review

The empirical foundation for my analysis is a large literature documenting
low inter-rater agreement in peer review across disciplines.
cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 conducted a
meta-analysis of 70 reliability coefficients from 48 studies covering 19,443
manuscripts. The headline finding: a mean intraclass correlation coefficient
(ICC) of 0.34 and a mean Cohen's kappa of 0.17. Only about one-third of the
variance in reviewer assessments reflects true quality differences. The
remaining two-thirds reflects reviewer disagreement.

Critically, this low agreement was consistent across /all/ disciplines
studied, including economics, law, natural sciences, medical sciences, and
social sciences. cite:hargensScholarlyConsensusJournal1988 argued that this
variation in agreement reflects variation in /scholarly consensus/: "When
scholars do not share conceptions of appropriate research problems,
theoretical approaches, or research techniques, they tend to view each
other's work as deficient and unworthy of publication." Low agreement is not
a problem of reviewer competence. It is an inherent feature of evaluating
work when there is no shared definition of quality.

This matters for accounting, where multiple methodological traditions
coexist (archival, experimental, analytical, qualitative) and where
cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 document widespread concern
that top journals favor narrow methodologies. If reviewers from different
methodological traditions disagree about what constitutes good research, the
effective ICC is lower than the cross-disciplinary average, amplifying
every problem I document. The $\sigma$ in my model represents precisely
this disagreement---the spread in reviewer assessments that arises from
lack of consensus, not from imprecise measurement of an objective quantity.

The phenomenon of reviewer disagreement has been recognized for decades.
cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981 studied 150 NSF proposals, each reviewed
by two independent panels, and famously concluded that "whether or not a
proposal is funded depends in a large proportion of cases upon which
reviewers happen to be selected for it." The same proposal, funded or
rejected, depending on who reads it.

** Prior Simulation Studies

Prior simulation studies have also examined peer review dysfunction.
cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006 characterized peer review as a "game of
chance," and cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017 found that papers at the 80th
percentile of quality face essentially coin-flip odds of acceptance---remarkably
consistent with my findings. I extend this work by integrating
multi-dimensional evaluation with empirical agreement calibration and by
deriving incentive implications from the resulting error structure.

** The Accounting Publication Context

Several studies document growing dissatisfaction with the accounting
publication process. cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016 compared
publication processes across accounting, economics, finance, management,
marketing, psychology, and natural sciences. Accounting academics perceived
very low citation patterns relative to other disciplines, believed the
review process was deteriorating, and desired acceptance rates roughly twice
as high as current levels.

cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024 updated this survey and found
perceptions have worsened since 2015. The rising generation of scholars
holds more negative views than their predecessors. Top journals favor
certain topic areas and methodologies; reviewers underweight practice
relevance; reviewers overweight incremental contribution and methodological
rigor. New assistant professors hold these views /more strongly/ than senior
faculty. These concerns appear to be intensifying over time.

The consequences extend beyond individual careers.
cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021 documented that accounting research
receives significantly less attention from policymakers and the general
public than economics, finance, and other disciplines. If reviewer
disagreement steers accounting research toward narrow, incremental
contributions---work where evaluation criteria are better established and
disagreement is lower---this would help explain the field's limited external
impact. The pattern Burton documents---top journals favoring narrow
methodologies---is exactly what one would expect if conformity is a rational
defense against subjectivity.

** The Research-Practice Gap

Concerns about accounting research relevance have deep roots.
cite:johnsonRelevanceLostRise1991 argued that management accounting had lost
its relevance to practitioners, becoming divorced from the information needs
of actual organizations. cite:kaplanAccountingScholarshipThat2011 called for
"accounting scholarship that advances professional knowledge and practice,"
arguing that the field had drifted toward technical exercises that fail to
inform either practitioners or policymakers.

More recent work quantifies the gap. cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021
assessed accounting research against three criteria: products and processes
produced, overlap with issues that concern CFOs and CEOs, and whether
academic know-how exceeds that of practitioners. His conclusion: academic
research has strayed from producing work useful to either practitioners or
policymakers, driven by the pressure to publish in elite journals.
cite:fraserAbundantPublicationsMinuscule2020 surveyed accounting
practitioners and found that academic research is largely ignored by the
profession---"abundant publications but minuscule impact."

cite:burtonRelevanceAccountingResearch2022 developed ROAR (Relevance of
Accounting Research) scores by having accounting professionals rate the
titles and abstracts of published papers. The results confirmed what many
suspected: much of what gets published in top journals is perceived as
irrelevant by those who might apply it. Combined with the attention data
from cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021, these findings point to a systematic
disconnect between what peer review rewards and what creates value for the
profession.

Reviewer disagreement offers an explanation for this gap. If reviewers
cannot agree on what "good" looks like for novel, practice-relevant
work---work that crosses traditional disciplinary boundaries and addresses
messy real-world problems---then rational researchers avoid it. Clustering
near established approaches, where evaluation criteria are better defined
and reviewer consensus is higher, becomes a survival strategy. Safe work
gets published. Useful work does not. The research-practice gap is a
predictable consequence of a system where subjectivity governs evaluation.

** Doctoral Socialization and Methodological Narrowing

A potential mechanism linking reviewer disagreement to research outcomes is
doctoral socialization. cite:fogartyHandThatRocks2010 examined the American
Accounting Association's Doctoral Consortium and found that "the most
important lesson about academic accounting may have been the righteousness
of the field's stratification hierarchy. In other words, learning one's
place in the US academy may be more important than any theory or technique
within academic accounting."

This makes sense if subjectivity is the core problem. More innovation means
more opportunity for reviewer disagreement. More disagreement means higher
rejection risk. Doctoral students learn---rationally---that success requires
conforming to what elite journals accept, because conformity reduces the
scope for disagreement. The system perpetuates itself: new faculty adopt
safe strategies, become reviewers who evaluate novel work against familiar
benchmarks, and train the next generation to do the same. Methodological
conformity is not timidity. It is a rational defense against a system where
reviewers cannot agree on what "good" looks like for work that breaks new
ground.

** Institutional Context: Accounting Journal Review Practices

Understanding the institutional structure of accounting journal review is
essential for calibrating my simulation and interpreting its implications.
The top accounting journals---The Accounting Review (TAR), Journal of
Accounting Research (JAR), and Journal of Accounting and Economics
(JAE)---differ meaningfully in their review processes, and all differ from
journals in other disciplines.

A striking feature of accounting is the small number of reviewers. TAR
typically uses two reviewers per submission. JAR and JAE often use just one.
In the natural sciences, three or more is common; in the medical sciences,
three to five. Yet accounting journals have /longer/ times to publication
than comparable journals in other fields
(cite:woodComparingPublicationProcess2016)---a pattern consistent with
reviewer disagreement driving multiple revision rounds. The analogy to
financial markets is instructive: just as disagreement among traders
generates trading volume and slow price convergence
(cite:kandelDifferentialInterpretationPublic1995), disagreement among
reviewers generates revision rounds and slow editorial convergence.

The single-reviewer model at JAR and JAE is particularly consequential for
my analysis. With one reviewer, there is no averaging of assessments---one
person's subjective assessment determines the paper's fate. JAR and JAE
incentivize fast turnaround by paying referees for reviews completed within
four weeks. This improves speed. It does nothing for agreement.

The institutional structure of accounting review creates conditions where
reviewer disagreement has maximum impact. One or two reviewers means
limited opportunity for divergent views to average out. The simulation
results I present below should be interpreted against this institutional
backdrop: accounting journals operate with fewer reviewers than most
disciplines, making them particularly vulnerable to the problems I
document.

** Interventions and Training

Can reviewer agreement be improved through training or procedural reforms?
cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023 conducted a systematic review
of ten randomized controlled trials examining reviewer training
interventions. Training produces "little or no improvement in peer review
quality." Structured review forms similarly fail to improve agreement.

This is exactly what the disagreement framing predicts. Training improves
competence, but lack of competence is not the problem. Reviewers already
read papers carefully and provide thoughtful feedback. They simply reach
different conclusions about what matters and what constitutes quality. You
cannot train people to agree when there is no ground truth to agree on.
Detailed rubrics do not help either---they merely formalize the dimensions
on which reviewers disagree.

The Spearman-Brown prophecy formula suggests that agreement improves with
additional reviewers, but with sharply diminishing returns: going from one
to two reviewers halves the disagreement variance, but going from four to
five reduces it by only 20%. My simulation quantifies these diminishing
returns and shows that meaningful improvements require structural changes
rather than marginal additions.

My simulation differs from prior work
(cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017; cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006) in integrating
multi-dimensional evaluation with empirical agreement calibration and in
calibrating to accounting-specific institutional features. It provides the
quantitative backbone for the mechanism emphasized here: structural
disagreement produces the publication dysfunction that the accounting
profession has long observed but misdiagnosed.

* A Model of Peer Review

** Setup

I model peer review as a signal extraction problem in which reviewer
subjectivity introduces substantial noise. A paper $i$ has
true quality on $D$ dimensions:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}_i = (q_{i1}, q_{i2}, \ldots, q_{iD})
\end{equation}
where each dimension might represent interest, rigor, contribution, or other
evaluation criteria. I assume true quality on each dimension is drawn from
a normal distribution:
\begin{equation}
q_{id} \sim N(\mu, \tau^2)
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is mean quality in the submission pool and $\tau^2$ is the
variance of true quality across papers.

** Reviewer Observation

Reviewer $j$ observes paper $i$'s quality on dimension $d$ with error:
\begin{equation}
\hat{q}_{ijd} = q_{id} + \epsilon_{ijd}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_{ijd} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$ is independent noise. Although
$\epsilon$ takes the standard statistical form of noise, it represents
something substantive: reviewer subjectivity. Reasonable, diligent reviewers
reach different conclusions about the same paper because there is no shared
definition of what "good" means on any given dimension. The parameter
$\sigma$ captures the magnitude of this disagreement.

When $n$ reviewers evaluate a paper, the editor aggregates their assessments
by averaging:
\begin{equation}
\bar{q}_{id} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\hat{q}_{ijd} = q_{id} + \bar{\epsilon}_{id}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\epsilon}_{id} \sim N(0, \sigma^2/n)$. Averaging reduces the
variance of disagreement by a factor of $n$, but with diminishing returns:
going from 1 to 2 reviewers halves the variance, but going from 4 to 5
reduces it by only 20%.

** Calibration to Empirical Agreement

The disagreement parameter $\sigma$ can be calibrated to empirical
inter-rater agreement data. The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) is
defined as:
\begin{equation}
ICC = \frac{\tau^2}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2}
\end{equation}
This represents the proportion of observed variance attributable to true
differences between papers rather than reviewer disagreement.

cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 conducted a
meta-analysis of 70 reliability coefficients from 48 studies covering 19,443
manuscripts across disciplines. They found a mean ICC of 0.34, indicating
that only about one-third of the variance in reviewer assessments reflects
true quality differences---the remaining two-thirds reflects reviewer
disagreement. Critically, this low agreement was consistent across /all/
disciplines studied, including economics, natural sciences, medical sciences,
and social sciences.

With ICC = 0.34 and assuming $\tau = 20$ (standard deviation of true quality
in the submission pool), I can solve for the disagreement parameter:
\begin{equation}
0.34 = \frac{400}{400 + \sigma^2} \implies \sigma^2 \approx 776 \implies \sigma \approx 28
\end{equation}
I use $\sigma = 30$ in my simulation, which is slightly conservative
(implying marginally more disagreement than the point estimate) and
simplifies interpretation.

** The AND-Gate Acceptance Rule

Journals typically require papers to meet standards on /multiple/
dimensions---a paper must be interesting /and/ rigorous /and/ well-written,
not merely good on average. I model this as a conjunctive decision rule,
which I term an "AND-gate": a paper is accepted if and only if its observed
quality exceeds the threshold $T$ on /all/ dimensions:
\begin{equation}
Accept_i = \mathbf{1}\left[\bar{q}_{id} \geq T \text{ for all } d \in \{1,\ldots,D\}\right]
\end{equation}

This AND-gate structure is what makes disagreement so damaging. Consider a
paper with true quality $q_{id} = T + \delta$ on all dimensions---a paper
that genuinely deserves acceptance by margin $\delta$. The probability that
reviewers' assessments fall below the threshold on any single dimension is:
\begin{equation}
P(\bar{q}_{id} < T) = \Phi\left(\frac{-\delta}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF.

With AND-gating across $D$ independent dimensions, the probability of
acceptance becomes:
\begin{equation}
P(Accept) = \left[1 - \Phi\left(\frac{-\delta}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^D
\end{equation}

Even if the probability of passing on each dimension is high, the joint
probability of passing on /all/ dimensions can be substantially lower.
Disagreement on /any/ dimension can sink a deserving paper. A paper that is
genuinely excellent can be rejected because one reviewer finds the topic
uninteresting while another finds the execution imperfect. Neither is
wrong. They simply disagree.

cite:bornmannReliabilityGeneralizationStudyJournal2010 found that
multi-dimensional rating systems are associated with /lower/ inter-rater
agreement, consistent with this theoretical prediction. More dimensions
mean more opportunities for disagreement to cause rejection.

** Correct Decision and Error Types

I define a paper as truly deserving acceptance if its true quality exceeds
the threshold on all dimensions:
\begin{equation}
ShouldAccept_i = \mathbf{1}\left[q_{id} \geq T \text{ for all } d \in \{1,\ldots,D\}\right]
\end{equation}

This yields four possible outcomes:
- *True Positive (TP):* Paper should be accepted and is accepted
- *False Positive (FP):* Paper should be rejected but is accepted
- *True Negative (TN):* Paper should be rejected and is rejected
- *False Negative (FN):* Paper should be accepted but is rejected

I focus particularly on the false negative rate (FNR):
\begin{equation}
FNR = \frac{FN}{FN + TP}
\end{equation}
This represents the proportion of deserving papers that are incorrectly
rejected---the rate at which good work is filtered out by reviewer
disagreement rather than quality.

** Why Simulation?

Before describing the simulation, it is worth explaining why simulation is
necessary rather than simply analyzing observational data on peer review
outcomes.

The fundamental challenge is that /true quality is unobserved/. Empirical
data can reveal what was accepted and rejected, but not what /should/ have
been accepted and rejected. Without knowing true quality, we cannot
calculate false positive or false negative rates---only acceptance rates.
Journals do not randomly assign reviewers, vary the number of reviewers
experimentally, or publish counterfactual decisions. Causal identification
of peer review mechanics is essentially impossible with observational data.

Simulation sidesteps this problem. Generate papers with /known/ true
quality, subject them to modeled review processes, and compare decisions to
ground truth. The cost is that results depend on modeling assumptions; the
benefit is that we can isolate mechanisms and examine counterfactuals that
could never be observed empirically. My approach calibrates key parameters
(especially the degree of reviewer disagreement) to empirical inter-rater
agreement data, grounding the simulation in observed reviewer behavior
while enabling analysis that observational data cannot support.

** Implementation

I simulate the model described in Section [[#model]]. For each experimental condition, I generate 1,000 papers with
true quality drawn independently on each dimension from $N(50, 20^2)$.
Reviewers observe each dimension with independent noise drawn from $N(0,
\sigma^2)$, and observations are averaged across reviewers. The editor
applies the AND-gate acceptance rule with threshold $T$.

I vary the following parameters:
- *Number of reviewers ($n$):* 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 10
- *Reviewer disagreement ($\sigma$):* 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40 (baseline 30; threshold interaction uses 15, 25, 35; quality/noise and crossover analyses use 15, 30, 45)
- *Number of dimensions ($D$):* 2, 3, 4
- *Threshold ($T$):* 40, 50, 60, 70

For each parameter combination, I run 50 independent replications (each
generating 1,000 papers) and report averaged results for stability.
Experiments that estimate acceptance probabilities at specific quality levels
use 10,000 Monte Carlo trials per condition.

The simulation code is implemented in Clojure and is publicly available at
https://github.com/drdebit/publication-sim for replication and extension.

** Baseline Parameters

My baseline parameters are:
- $n = 2$ reviewers (typical for accounting journals)
- $\sigma = 30$ (calibrated to ICC = 0.34)
- $D = 2$ dimensions (interest and rigor)
- $T = 50$ (moderate selectivity)

I then vary each parameter to examine main effects and interactions.

** Design Choices and Assumptions

Several modeling choices warrant explicit justification.

/Dimensionality./ I use 2--4 evaluation dimensions to capture the range of
journal practices. Two dimensions---typically "interest/contribution" and
"execution/rigor"---represent the minimal evaluation structure common to most
journals. Three and four dimensions capture more elaborate rubrics that
additionally separate theoretical contribution, empirical execution, and
writing quality. Equal weighting across dimensions is a simplifying assumption;
in practice, editors may weight dimensions differently. However, equal
weighting is conservative: if one dimension receives low weight but still
operates as a binding constraint (a paper cannot be accepted with fatal
execution flaws regardless of contribution), the AND-gate effect would be
amplified rather than attenuated.

/Symmetric thresholds./ I apply the same threshold to all dimensions. This
simplifies analysis and reflects the intuition that journals have minimum
standards on each criterion. Asymmetric thresholds would change the relative
frequency of rejection reasons but not the fundamental AND-gate dynamic.

/Independence across dimensions./ I assume true quality is independent across
dimensions. In reality, quality may be positively correlated (good researchers
produce papers that are strong on multiple dimensions). Positive correlation
would reduce the AND-gate effect by making it less likely that a paper is
strong on one dimension but weak on another. Simulation with modest positive
correlation ($\rho_{dim} = 0.4$) shows that baseline FNR falls from
approximately 45% to approximately 42%---attenuated but still substantial. My
independence assumption is thus conservative---it produces higher false
rejection rates than would obtain under positive correlation.

/Independence of reviewer errors./ My baseline assumes reviewer errors are
independent, which I relax in the robustness analysis. As shown in Section
[[#robustness]], correlated errors can substantially attenuate the benefit of
additional reviewers.

** Main Finding: False Rejection Rates

| Reviewers | FNR    | FPR    | Accuracy | Reliability |
|-----------+--------+--------+----------+-------------|
|         1 | 51.9%  |  17.6% |    73.8% |       55.8% |
|         2 | 45.0%  |  15.0% |    77.4% |       68.5% |
|         3 | 38.8%  |  13.2% |    80.5% |       75.3% |
|         4 | 37.6%  |  12.4% |    81.4% |       80.2% |
|         5 | 34.5%  |  11.1% |    83.1% |       83.2% |
|         7 | 30.0%  |   9.9% |    85.1% |       86.6% |
|        10 | 27.7%  |   9.3% |    86.3% |       90.2% |

Reliability denotes the correlation between true total quality and observed
total quality across all papers---i.e., how well the review process
preserves the rank ordering of manuscripts.

With two reviewers---the accounting norm---nearly half of deserving papers
are rejected (FNR = 0.45). Not because they are bad. Because reviewers
disagree.

The gains from additional reviewers are real but diminishing. Going from
one to two reviewers reduces FNR by 6.9 percentage points---the largest
single improvement. Going from two to five provides another 10.5 points.
Beyond five, gains are marginal: doubling the reviewer count from 5 to 10
buys 6.8 points.

These magnitudes are consistent with cite:esareyDoesPeerReview2017, who
find that papers at the 80th percentile of quality face essentially
coin-flip odds of acceptance. My simulation provides the mechanism: low
inter-rater agreement combined with multi-dimensional evaluation.

The single-reviewer case deserves particular attention. JAR and JAE
frequently use one reviewer. With a single reviewer, FNR rises to 52%---more
than half of deserving papers rejected on one person's subjective
assessment. Adding a second reviewer is the largest marginal gain in the
table. Yet these journals have chosen speed over accuracy.

** The AND-Gate Effect: Dimensions Amplify Errors

#+CAPTION: Effect of Evaluation Dimensions (AND-gating)
| Dimensions | FNR    | FPR   | Accuracy |
|------------+--------+-------+----------|
|          2 | 45.3%  | 15.3% |    77.1% |
|          3 | 60.0%  |  8.5% |    85.1% |
|          4 | 70.5%  |  4.5% |    91.6% |

Adding evaluation dimensions substantially increases false rejection rates.
With two dimensions, FNR is about 45%; with three, 60%; with four, 71%.

Each additional dimension creates another potential failure point under a
conjunctive rule. When acceptance requires clearing the threshold on every
dimension, disagreement on any single dimension can produce rejection. The
AND-gate is therefore most punitive for papers near the threshold, which are
also the papers most selective journals are attempting to distinguish.

Four evaluation dimensions is not implausible. Referee reports typically
include multiple distinct judgments---interest, rigor, contribution, writing
quality, data quality, and theoretical framing. If these operate as binding
constraints, the AND-gate effect follows.

lenient journals, deserving and undeserving papers are better separated,

move in opposite directions.

** Disagreement Helps Bad Papers, Hurts Good Papers

#+CAPTION: The lines cross at the threshold: disagreement systematically redistributes acceptance from deserving to undeserving papers.

#+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.85\textwidth :placement [htbp]

Figure [[fig:quality-noise]] shows that disagreement systematically shifts
acceptance probability across the quality distribution. At quality = 90,
acceptance probability falls from 94% under low disagreement to 52% under
high disagreement. At quality = 50, acceptance probability rises from 0.2%
to 7%. Disagreement therefore reduces acceptance for high-quality papers and
increases acceptance for lower-quality papers.

The lines converge near the threshold (quality = 70), where acceptance
probability is roughly 25% regardless of disagreement level. For borderline
papers, disagreement does not change acceptance much; for very strong
papers, it substantially reduces acceptance probabilities.

*** The Threshold as Crossover Point

To understand this redistribution more precisely, I examine how the effect of
disagreement varies across the full quality spectrum.

| Quality | P(Low Disagr.) | P(High Disagr.) |  Delta | Effect  |
|---------+----------------+-----------------+--------+---------|
|      50 |           0.1% |            6.9% |  +6.8% | Helps   |

|      64 |           7.0% |           18.3% | +11.2% | Helps   |

|      90 |          94.4% |           53.3% | -41.1% | Hurts   |

The threshold is a crossover point. Below the threshold, disagreement
increases acceptance probabilities by occasionally pushing papers above the
bar. The maximum benefit occurs around 6--12 points below threshold (quality
58--64), where high disagreement increases acceptance by roughly 11
percentage points. Above the threshold, disagreement reduces acceptance, and
the reduction grows with quality; at quality 90, high disagreement reduces
acceptance by more than 40 percentage points.

This asymmetry reflects variance around true quality. Below threshold,
upward variance helps while downward variance does not change the decision.
Above threshold, downward variance hurts while upward variance does not
change the decision. The net effect is a redistribution of acceptance from
high-quality to lower-quality papers.

*** Convergence to Mediocrity

This redistribution has a profound implication: disagreement pulls the
/published/ literature toward mediocrity from both ends of the quality
distribution.

Disagreement does not change true quality---it changes /observed/ quality.
Some excellent papers are observed as merely good and rejected. Some
mediocre papers are observed as acceptable and published. The threshold acts
as an attractor: with sufficient disagreement, acceptance rates across
quality levels converge toward whatever baseline rate exists at the
threshold.

Consider the compression under high disagreement:
- Quality 90 → 53% acceptance (down from 94% with low disagreement)
- Quality 50 → 7% acceptance (up from 0.1% with low disagreement)

The ratio of acceptance rates shrinks from 940:1 to 8:1. High disagreement
therefore weakens the relationship between true quality and publication.
The published literature becomes less representative of underlying quality
differences and more sensitive to noise in reviewer assessments.

From the perspective of published output, the quality distribution appears
narrower than the submission distribution: the tails are truncated from both
ends. Some excellent papers are rejected, and some mediocre papers are
accepted. The published record therefore underrepresents the true variance
in research quality.

This compression provides a mechanism for perceptions of increasing
similarity in top journals. Disagreement compresses the distribution of
accepted papers, and correlated assessments among similar reviewers can
reinforce this effect (see Section [[#robustness]]). The resulting output
clusters in a narrow band of "safe" work, consistent with the
methodological narrowing documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024.

This mechanism does not depend on reviewer intent. Even with diligent,
competent reviewers, sufficiently high disagreement implies convergence in
the observed quality of accepted papers.

quality, because marginal quality above the threshold yields diminishing
returns.

*** Implications for Researcher Behavior

If researchers respond to these incentives, the pattern is consistent with
several observed phenomena:
- The prevalence of "salami-slicing" (fragmenting research into minimal
  publishable units)
- Field wisdom to "write twice as many papers as you think you need"
- The trend toward more incremental contributions noted by
  cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024
- The observation that most papers aim for "good enough" rather than
  "exceptional"

A methodological note: I do not model researcher behavior directly. The
simulation calculates acceptance probabilities at different quality levels,
not equilibrium strategies that account for effort costs, career dynamics,
co-authorship, or multi-period optimization. The strategy comparison is
/comparative statics/---how expected publications vary with quality and
quantity choices---not predictions about actual behavior. A full equilibrium
model would require additional assumptions beyond this paper's scope.

The incentive gradients are consistent with observed behavior and with
cite:azoulayDoesPeerReview2025, who provide evidence that peer review
penalizes researchers who pursue bold, unconventional work. If innovative
research faces higher variance in reviewer assessments---less consensus on
what "good" looks like---the AND-gate effect is amplified, making
conservative, incremental work relatively more attractive in expected value
terms.

*** The Clustering Strategy: Conformity as Rational Defense

The incentive results above address effort allocation---how many papers to
write at what quality. But there is a complementary strategy: reduce the
/scope for disagreement/ itself.

Since FNR rises with $\sigma$ (the disagreement parameter), anything that
lowers the effective $\sigma$ for a given paper reduces its rejection risk.
A researcher cannot change the /system's/ ICC. But a researcher can choose
to work in areas where reviewer agreement is /higher/---established
methodologies, well-defined research questions, incremental extensions of
existing work. In these areas, reviewers have clearer benchmarks for
"good," and the effective disagreement is lower. (The ICC sensitivity
analysis in Section [[#robustness]] quantifies this: FNR drops from 52% at
ICC = 0.20 to 36% at ICC = 0.50.)

This rationalizes the methodological conformity documented throughout the
literature. Clustering near established approaches is not timidity or lack
of imagination. It is a rational defense against a system where subjectivity
governs outcomes. Innovation increases the space for disagreement.
Conformity reduces it.

The consequences are individually rational but collectively harmful:
- The research-practice gap widens because safe work is not useful work
  (cite:rajgopalIntegratingPracticeAccounting2021)
- Methodological narrowing intensifies because each generation of
  conformists trains the next (cite:fogartyHandThatRocks2010)
- The field's external impact declines because incremental extensions of
  established approaches do not address the questions practitioners care
  about (cite:burtonWeMatterAttention2021)

The clustering strategy explains why common reforms---reviewer training,
detailed rubrics, exhortations to value innovation---fail. These reforms do
not address the underlying incentive: conformity /works/ because it reduces
disagreement. Until the system is restructured so that disagreement does not
determine outcomes, rational researchers will continue to cluster.

** Implications

*** For Editors and Journals

These results suggest several directions for editors seeking to improve
decision quality.

*Reviewer quantity and diversity.* The largest accuracy gains come from going
from 1 to 2 reviewers, but meaningful improvements continue through 3-5
reviewers. If accounting journals typically use 1-2 reviewers, there is
accuracy left on the table. However, the correlated-errors analysis (Section [[#robustness]]) indicates
that reviewer /diversity/ may matter more than reviewer /quantity/. Adding a
third reviewer from the same methodological tradition provides less benefit
than adding a reviewer with different training. Editors might therefore
prioritize methodologically diverse review teams rather than simply adding
reviewers.

*Decision rule architecture.* Comprehensive rubrics with many evaluation
criteria /reduce/ decision quality by creating more opportunities for
disagreement to reject good papers. Journals should consider compensatory
rather than conjunctive decision rules---allowing excellence on one
dimension to offset weakness on another---or reducing the number of explicit
evaluation dimensions. Requiring papers to "pass" on interest, rigor,
contribution, and writing creates four independent opportunities for
disagreement to sink a deserving paper.

*Transparent acknowledgment of uncertainty.* Roughly half of rejection
decisions for borderline-good papers are determined by reviewer disagreement
rather than true quality. Journals could acknowledge this in rejection
letters, distinguishing between "clear reject" (paper unlikely to succeed
anywhere) and "unlucky reject" (paper might well have been accepted with
different reviewers). This transparency could reduce author frustration and encourage appropriate
resubmission behavior.

*Structural alternatives.* Given that training doesn't improve agreement,
journals might explore structural changes. Registered reports separate
evaluation of research questions from evaluation of results, potentially
reducing disagreement from hindsight bias. Multi-stage review that filters
on different dimensions sequentially rather than conjunctively could improve
accuracy for papers that clear initial screens.

*Experimentation and measurement.* Journals could measure their own
agreement levels by occasionally having papers reviewed by independent
panels, as in cite:coleChanceConsensusPeer1981. This would provide
field-specific agreement estimates and allow journals to evaluate whether
interventions actually improve decision quality. Without measurement,
improvement is difficult.

*** Why Common Intuitions Fail

Several of these implications are counterintuitive. Why do common-sense
approaches to improving peer review backfire?

/More detailed rubrics do not help./ Each additional criterion is another
dimension on which reviewers can disagree. AND-gating turns comprehensive
evaluation into a liability.

/Adding reviewers has sharply diminishing returns./ Under averaging,
agreement improves at $1/\sqrt{n}$. Going from 2 to 10 reviewers improves
accuracy by about 9 percentage points, often at high cost.

/Reviewer training does not improve agreement./ Low agreement reflects lack
of consensus about quality, not reviewer incompetence
(cite:hesselbergReviewerTrainingImproving2023).

/Transparency does not automatically improve outcomes./ The central issue
is disagreement, not effort. Reviewers who try harder still disagree.

These failures share a common root: they treat peer review as a solvable
measurement problem. Once we accept that reviewers genuinely disagree about
what constitutes quality, attention shifts from "fixing reviewers" to
designing systems that are robust to disagreement.

*** For Researchers

The field wisdom that good papers have roughly 50-50 odds at top journals
is consistent with this model. Rejection is a noisy signal. A rejection
from one journal provides limited information about likely success at
another with different reviewers.

Persistence in resubmission is therefore rational.
cite:neffPeerReviewGame2006 show that resubmission strategies can
significantly boost publication chances. But this creates its own
inefficiency: reviewer effort is duplicated, and persistence rather than
quality becomes a determinant of publication.

When interpreting feedback, the degree of agreement among reviewers
matters. Detailed, specific critiques are more likely to contain signal.
Vague objections to "contribution" or "interest" may reflect taste
differences rather than fundamental problems.

*** For the Profession

Beyond individual journals and researchers, these findings raise broader
concerns about knowledge production in accounting.

*Talent allocation.* High false rejection rates impose costs beyond
frustration. Talented researchers who experience repeated rejection may exit
academia or redirect effort toward consulting, teaching, or administration.
If rejection is partly driven by disagreement, some of those who exit are
high-quality researchers who drew unsympathetic reviewers. The profession
loses their contributions.

*Credentialing vs. knowledge production.* There is a tension between peer
review's credentialing function (certifying researcher quality for tenure
and promotion) and its knowledge production function (filtering ideas to
advance understanding). A credentialing system plagued by disagreement may
still "work" if randomness averages out across careers. A knowledge filter
plagued by disagreement fails directly: good ideas are rejected, resources
are wasted on duplicated review effort, and incremental work crowds out
innovation.

*Collective action problems.* Individual journals face limited incentive to
invest in agreement improvements. Adding reviewers is costly. Measuring
agreement is effort-intensive. Journals compete on prestige, not decision
accuracy. Improving the system therefore requires coordinated action---from
professional associations, from funders who could require agreement
reporting, or from researchers who could collectively demand better
processes.

*Alternative models.* Other fields have experimented with alternatives:
open review (where reviews are published), post-publication review (where
filtering happens after publication), preprint servers (which separate
dissemination from certification), and overlay journals (which curate rather
than gatekeep). Accounting has been conservative in adopting these models.
The costs of the traditional model are higher than commonly recognized. The
field should be experimenting.

** Correlated Reviewer Errors

My baseline assumes reviewer errors are independent---one reviewer's
assessment does not influence another's. This assumption may be violated if:
(1) reviewers share methodological training that biases them similarly; (2)
reviewers observe each other's assessments in sequential review; or (3)
reviewers draw on common information (e.g., the same literature) that
systematically misleads them.

I model correlated errors by decomposing each reviewer's noise into a shared
component and an independent component: $\epsilon_{ijd} = \sqrt{\rho}\,\eta_{id} + \sqrt{1-\rho}\,\nu_{ijd}$,
where $\eta_{id}$ is shared across reviewers and $\nu_{ijd}$ is independent.
This produces equicorrelated noise with pairwise correlation $\rho$ between reviewers.
Note that this captures correlation /across reviewers/---the tendency for
different reviewers to err in the same direction on the same paper---rather
than correlation /within a reviewer across dimensions/ (e.g., a harsh reviewer
being harsh on all criteria). The latter would affect how dimensions combine
but not how reviewers aggregate; the former directly undermines the benefit of
multiple reviewers.

#+CAPTION: Effect of Correlated Reviewer Errors
| Correlation | Reviewers | Accuracy | FNR    |
|-------------+-----------+----------+--------|
|        0.00 |         2 |    77.9% | 43.9%  |
|        0.00 |         3 |    80.1% | 39.9%  |
|        0.00 |         5 |    83.4% | 33.9%  |
|        0.25 |         2 |    76.9% | 46.3%  |
|        0.25 |         3 |    78.5% | 43.2%  |
|        0.25 |         5 |    80.2% | 39.9%  |
|        0.50 |         2 |    75.0% | 49.9%  |
|        0.50 |         3 |    76.0% | 49.0%  |
|        0.50 |         5 |    76.6% | 47.3%  |
|        0.75 |         2 |    75.2% | 50.5%  |
|        0.75 |         3 |    75.5% | 49.7%  |
|        0.75 |         5 |    75.5% | 49.7%  |

Correlated errors substantially reduce the benefit of additional reviewers.
At $\rho = 0$ (independent errors), going from 2 to 5 reviewers improves
accuracy from 77.9% to 83.4%, a gain of 5.5 percentage points. At
$\rho = 0.75$ (highly correlated errors), the same change yields essentially
no improvement (75.2% to 75.5%).

If reviewers share a methodological worldview that disadvantages certain
research approaches, adding more reviewers from the same pool is unlikely to
improve accuracy.

The independent-errors baseline remains empirically relevant. As discussed
in the introduction, correlated biases (high $\rho$) would produce quick,
consistent decisions. Instead, we observe long publication times, multiple
revision rounds, and papers rejected at one journal accepted at another with
different reviewers. These patterns are consistent with independent errors.

Accordingly, the correlated-errors analysis is best interpreted as a
/counterfactual/: what would happen if reviewers agreed. Under such
conditions, adding reviewers would provide little benefit but decisions
would be faster and more consistent. The main results are grounded in the
independent-errors case.

** Sensitivity to Quality Variance

My baseline assumes true quality has standard deviation $\tau = 20$. This
parameter affects both the difficulty of discrimination (smaller $\tau$ means
papers are more similar) and the calibrated disagreement level (since ICC depends on
both $\tau$ and $\sigma$).

#+CAPTION: Sensitivity to Quality Variance (τ)
| τ  | FNR    | Accuracy |
|----+--------+----------|
| 15 | 52.9%  |    74.0% |
| 20 | 43.6%  |    78.1% |
| 25 | 38.6%  |    80.7% |
| 30 | 33.9%  |    83.1% |

Lower quality variance (τ = 15) increases FNR to 52.9%; higher variance (τ
= 30) reduces it to 33.9%. Two reasons. First, with less variance in true
quality, papers cluster more tightly near the threshold, where disagreement
is most likely to cause misclassification. Second, at fixed ICC, lower
$\tau$ implies lower $\sigma$ (less absolute disagreement), but the relative
disagreement compared to quality differences remains constant.

The implication is that if accounting submissions exhibit relatively
homogeneous quality---most papers from qualified researchers meeting
professional standards---the effective false rejection rate is higher than
my baseline estimate. If quality is highly variable, discrimination is
easier than the model suggests.

** Theoretical Considerations: Non-Normal Distributions

My model assumes normally distributed true quality and reviewer
disagreement. Empirical quality distributions may differ: true quality might
be right-skewed (most papers are mediocre, few are excellent) or
heavy-tailed (occasional breakthrough papers).

I do not simulate alternative distributions, but their likely effects are
clear. Right-skewed quality distributions place more papers near the left
tail, where disagreement is more likely to generate false /positives/ than
false negatives. This could mitigate FNR concerns for the typical paper
while exacerbating FPR concerns. Heavy-tailed disagreement distributions
(e.g., occasional highly discordant reviewers) would reduce the
effectiveness of averaging and could favor median aggregation.

The baseline estimates assume symmetric, well-behaved distributions.
Departures from normality could amplify or attenuate specific effects, but
the fundamental tension between selectivity and agreement remains.

the majority of deserving papers face rejection. The baseline estimate of ~45%
should be interpreted as a central estimate within a range of roughly 28--55%
depending on institutional features and true agreement levels.

Across sensitivity analyses, several findings are robust:

1. *High false rejection rates persist.* Even at the most optimistic ICC (0.50)
   and highest quality variance (τ = 30), FNR exceeds 33%. Under pessimistic
   but plausible assumptions, FNR exceeds 50%.

2. *Diminishing returns to reviewers.* Going from 1 to 2 reviewers provides the
   largest benefit; going beyond 5 provides marginal improvement. This finding
   is robust across ICC values and aggregation methods.

3. *Correlated errors undermine reviewer aggregation.* If reviewers share
   systematic biases, adding reviewers provides little benefit. This suggests
   reviewer diversity may matter more than reviewer quantity.

4. *Perverse incentives persist.* The incentive to produce multiple
   moderate-quality papers rather than single excellent papers emerges from
   stochastic acceptance, which is present regardless of specific parameter
   values.

The qualitative conclusions are robust. The quantitative estimates depend
on parameters that are imperfectly known, and should be interpreted
accordingly.

* Limitations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: limitations
:END:

My simulation makes simplifying assumptions. Reviewer disagreement is
independent and identically distributed; in reality, reviewers may have
correlated biases. Dimensions are independent; in reality, true quality may
be correlated across dimensions. Aggregation is by simple averaging; in
reality, editors may weight reviewers differently.

These simplifications likely make the estimates /conservative/. Correlated
reviewer biases would not average out as effectively. Positive dimension
correlation would /reduce/ the AND-gate effect (a paper strong on one
dimension is likely strong on others), attenuating false rejection rates.
Non-uniform weighting could help or hurt depending on whether editors
correctly identify reliable reviewers.

I also do not model the full publication ecosystem, including resubmission
to other journals, desk rejection, or strategic author behavior. Future work
could extend the simulation to capture these dynamics.

Finally, I model the editor as mechanically applying a conjunctive threshold,
but real editors exercise discretion. An experienced editor may override a
split decision, weigh one reviewer's expertise more heavily, or recognize
when a negative review reflects taste rather than quality. Such discretion
could partially mitigate the AND-gate effect if editors systematically
correct for disagreement. However, discretion could also introduce new
biases. The net effect is an empirical question that the simulation does
not address.

* Conclusion
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: conclusion
:END:

I model peer review as a system in which reviewer disagreement---not
bias---drives outcomes, and use simulation to examine the consequences.
Calibrating to empirical inter-rater agreement data (ICC = 0.34), I find
that approximately 45% of deserving papers are rejected due to reviewer
disagreement alone. Low agreement, multi-dimensional evaluation, and high
selectivity combine to produce a system with high false rejection rates.

The problem is not biased reviewers. It is structural disagreement
operating through a decision architecture that amplifies its effects.
Training does not work because the problem is not competence. Detailed
rubrics do not work because they add dimensions on which reviewers
disagree. What works: fewer evaluation dimensions, compensatory decision
rules, reviewer diversity, and explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty.

Disagreement creates incentives toward quantity over quality. Researchers
maximize expected publications by producing multiple moderate-quality papers
rather than a single excellent paper; "two very good" dominates "one
excellent" by 46% in expected output. Methodological conformity also becomes
a rational defense, because clustering near established approaches reduces
the scope for disagreement and lowers rejection risk. These incentives are
consistent with the prevalence of incremental research, the research-practice
gap, and methodological narrowing documented in the literature.

** Future Research Directions

Several extensions would strengthen this analysis.

First, accounting-specific agreement data---obtained by having papers
reviewed by independent panels---would allow more precise estimates of
false rejection rates. Such data could also test whether agreement varies
by methodology, topic area, or paper type.

Second, modeling the full publication ecosystem would capture dynamics I
abstract away. Papers rejected at one journal are revised and resubmitted
elsewhere; modeling this process would reveal system-level outcomes
(eventual publication rates, total reviewer effort) rather than
single-journal outcomes.

Third, my model treats quality as exogenous, but researchers choose how to
allocate effort. An equilibrium model could reveal feedback effects I do not
capture. If disagreement-driven review induces quantity-over-quality and
clustering strategies, the resulting submissions become more homogeneous in
quality (lower $\tau$), which my sensitivity analysis suggests would
/increase/ false rejection rates, further strengthening incentives for
conformity. The system could converge to a low-quality, high-conformity
equilibrium even if it began with higher-quality, more diverse submissions.

Fourth, comparative analysis across fields could test whether accounting's
institutional features---few reviewers, long review times, high rejection
rates---produce worse outcomes than alternative configurations. Natural
experiments, such as journals that changed reviewer counts or decision
processes, could provide causal evidence.

Finally, field experiments would move from diagnosis to treatment. Journals
could randomly assign papers to different numbers of reviewers, different
decision rules, or different reviewer selection procedures, measuring
effects on eventual outcomes (citation, replication, practitioner impact)
rather than just immediate acceptance decisions.

** Broader Implications

These mechanisms are not specific to accounting. Peer review is the
primary quality filter for scholarly knowledge across disciplines, and
reviewer agreement is consistently low. The same dynamics---high false
rejection, perverse incentives, convergence to mediocrity---operate
wherever reviewers disagree and decisions are conjunctive.

The downstream consequences compound. Funders, universities, and
governments rely on publication records to allocate resources, grant tenure,
and assess research impact. If publication outcomes are substantially driven
by reviewer disagreement, those downstream decisions inherit the
arbitrariness.

Peer review emerged in an era of scarce publication capacity. In the
digital era, those constraints have relaxed, yet pre-digital filtering
mechanisms remain. Post-publication review, open access archives, and
algorithmic curation offer alternatives that may better balance quality
control against the costs of false rejection. I do not advocate abandoning
peer review, which provides valuable functions beyond filtering, but these
results suggest that experimentation with alternatives deserves serious
consideration.

** Closing Thoughts

The frustration documented by cite:burtonPerceptionsAccountingAcademics2024
is well documented. Accounting researchers report that the publication
process rejects good work, favors narrow approaches, and rewards the wrong
behaviors. These perceptions are consistent with the structural consequences
of reviewer disagreement.

Attributing dysfunction primarily to bias can misdirect reform efforts.
Bias is addressable through training, diversification, and vigilance.
Disagreement requires structural change---redesigning the decision
architecture so that divergent reviewer assessments do not mechanically
determine outcomes. If reforms focus on bias while disagreement drives
outcomes, improvements will be limited.

For accounting research to matter to practitioners, policymakers, and the
public, publication systems must more reliably identify and disseminate good
work. Building such systems is an important challenge for the profession.
